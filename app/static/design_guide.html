Designing for Circularity: A Practitioner's Guide

Atlas Circular · Circularity Intelligence · Compiled 30 June 2026

How to design packaging and products that stay compliant — and pay lower fees — under US Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) and adjacent circular-economy law.

Derived from 471 bills across 35 states (456 already enacted) that our system has classified and analyzed. Every principle below traces to specific bills, with the exact statutory language quoted. Proposed bills show the direction of travel; enacted laws are obligations today.

How to use this guide. Each lever is a design decision you control. For every lever: the *imperatives* are what to do, *targets* are the hard numbers to hit, and *evidence* shows the laws driving it. Audit your SKUs lever by lever; prioritize the states you sell into. Fee figures are intentionally omitted until the Circular Action Alliance publishes eco-modulation rates — at which point design choices map directly to dollar exposure.

Part 1 — Packaging design

Design packaging to be recyclable in available systems

Incorporate post-consumer recycled content

Incorporating post-consumer recycled (PCR) content is a cross-sector design obligation driven by enacted law across packaging, carpet, and durable goods categories. Mandatory minimums are already in force or on near-term timelines, and eco-modulated fee structures reward higher PCR rates—making early adoption both a compliance necessity and a cost-reduction opportunity.

Backed by 71 bills (67 enacted) — Required in 14 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 7 state(s) · Referenced in 4 state(s) · Exemption available in 1 state(s) · Prohibited in 1 state(s) · Fee-penalized in 1 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Hit staged PCR minimums in all single-use plastic beverage bottles now (required). PET beverage bottles must contain ≥25% recycled plastic content from 2025, rising to ≥30% for all single-use plastic beverage bottles from 2030; calculate compliance as an annual average across the product line. — AT 20008902, PL DU/2023/1658, PL DU/2024/927, SE sfs-2018-1462
  • Ramp carpet PCR content across three mandatory milestones (required). Carpet must be manufactured with ≥10% post-consumer content at Year 2, ≥20% at Year 6, and ≥30% at Year 11 after the effective date; build supplier contracts and fiber-sourcing programs now to meet each step-up. — NY S-5027C
  • Embed recycled plastic targets in all plastic-heavy packaging designs (required). Packaging containing more than 50% plastic must effectively contribute to a ≥30% recycled plastic content target by 2030; align material specifications and supplier qualification to this threshold across the portfolio. — SE sfs-2018-1462, EU 32025R0040
  • Set and publish recycled-plastic utilization targets for consumer electronics and appliances (required). Manufacturers of automobiles, air conditioners, televisions, refrigerators, and washing machines must establish planned targets for recycled-plastic usage volume and mass ratio within technically and economically feasible limits; document these targets formally and track progress annually. — JP 508M60000400007, JP 508M60000400008, JP 508M60000400009, JP 508M60000400010, JP 508M60000400011
  • Maximize recycled content in packaging to reduce eco-modulated fees (fee-advantaged). Extended-producer-responsibility fee schedules are modulated to reward increased post-consumer recycled content in packaging materials; design teams should specify the highest feasible PCR rate to minimize fee liability. — MN HF-3911, ES BOE-A-2022-5809
  • Restrict recycled-material sourcing in plastic crates and pallets to closed-loop streams (required). Recycled material incorporated into plastic crates or pallets must originate only from other plastic crates or pallets; introduction of any other material must not exceed 20% by weight. — EU 32009D0292

Targets to hit

  • ≥25% recycled plastic in PET single-use beverage bottles from 2025 (AT 20008902, PL DU/2023/1658, PL DU/2024/927, SE sfs-2018-1462)
  • ≥30% recycled plastic in all single-use plastic beverage bottles from 2030 (AT 20008902, PL DU/2023/1658, PL DU/2024/927, SE sfs-2018-1462)
  • ≥10% post-consumer recycled content in carpet at Year 2 (NY S-5027C)
  • ≥20% post-consumer recycled content in carpet at Year 6 (NY S-5027C)
  • ≥30% post-consumer recycled content in carpet at Year 11 (NY S-5027C)
  • ≥30% recycled plastic in packaging containing >50% plastic by 2030 (SE sfs-2018-1462)
  • ≤20% by weight non-crate/pallet material in recycled content for plastic crates and pallets (EU 32009D0292)
  • ≥50% of waste paper sourced from offices/businesses or new previously uncollected sources (EU 32008D0440)
Evidence (statutory language)

— AT 20008902 (enacted, Required)
"From 2030: ensure all single-use plastic beverage bottles (Annex 6 Point 3) contain on average at least 30% recycled plastic content (calculated per calendar year)"
— AT 20008902 (enacted, Required)
"Ensure carpet is manufactured with a minimum of 10% post-consumer content (effective two years after effective date)"
— NY S-5027C (enacted, Required)

Reduce packaging material per unit (lightweight, right-size)

Source reduction requires designing packaging and products to use the minimum material necessary — eliminating unnecessary components, right-sizing dimensions, and lightweighting structures. Multiple enacted laws across the EU, France, Estonia, Finland, Lithuania, and Japan mandate this as a baseline obligation, making it a non-negotiable design starting point rather than an aspirational goal.

Backed by 138 bills (136 enacted) — Required in 20 state(s) · Fee-penalized in 10 state(s) · Referenced in 10 state(s) · Exemption available in 8 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 5 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Minimize packaging volume and weight to the adequate functional minimum (required). At structural design stage, benchmark every packaging format against the minimum volume and weight that still meets safety, hygiene, transport, and regulatory-information requirements — any excess must be removed before tooling is finalized. — EU 32025R0040, EE 113032019103, LT TAIS.161216
  • Eliminate packaging components that serve no essential technical function (required). Audit each layer, insert, sleeve, and secondary pack against a defined list of essential functions (protection, sanitary integrity, transport, regulatory information); remove any component that fails this test, targeting full elimination of non-functional single-use plastic elements by 31 December 2025. — EU 32025R0040, FR JORFTEXT000043458675, FI 2011/646, FI 2021/714
  • Right-size single-use plastic beverage bottle formats to reduce units placed on market (required). For beverage product lines, redesign fill volumes, concentrate formats, or refill systems to contribute to a 50% reduction in the number of single-use plastic beverage bottles placed on the French market by 2030. — FR cenv/L541-10-11
  • Adopt compact or lightweight housings, chassis, and component parts in electronics and appliances (required). For consumer electronics, appliances, game hardware, and vehicles, rationalize raw material use by specifying smaller or lighter housings, compressors, drive units, transmissions, and structural parts — reducing end-of-life material mass at the product design stage. — JP 413M60000400062, JP 413M60000400063, JP 413M60000400069, JP 413M60000400075, JP 504M60007FFE001, JP 413M60000C00004
  • Simplify packaging design and substitute plastic with alternative materials in distribution (required). In distribution and sales operations, promote simplified packaging formats and actively substitute plastic packaging materials with non-plastic alternatives wherever technically feasible. — JP 504M60007FFE001
  • Cap ready-to-sell consumer package weight at 50 kg per unit (required). Any product packaged for direct sale to the final consumer must be presented in ready-to-sell packages not exceeding 50 kg in weight; verify compliance at packaging specification sign-off. — EU 32021R1929

Targets to hit

  • 50% reduction in number of single-use plastic beverage bottles placed on market by 2030 (FR cenv/L541-10-11)
  • 100% elimination of unnecessary single-use plastic packaging lacking essential technical functions by 31 December 2025 (FR JORFTEXT000043458675)
  • Maximum 50 kg per ready-to-sell consumer package unit (EU 32021R1929)
Evidence (statutory language)
"Such products must be packaged in ready-to-sell packages of not more than 50 kg in weight for use by the final consumer"
— EU 32021R1929 (enacted, Required)
"Producers must contribute to a 50% reduction by 2030 in the number of single-use plastic beverage bottles placed on the market"
— FR cenv/L541-10-11 (enacted, Required)
"Tend toward 100% elimination of unnecessary single-use plastic packaging (those lacking essential technical functions such as protection, sanitary/integrity, transport, or regulatory information support) by 31 December 2025"
— FR JORFTEXT000043458675 (enacted, Required)

Shift to reusable / refillable formats

The reuse/refill lever shifts product and packaging design away from single-use formats toward systems that complete multiple life cycles, reducing material throughput and EPR obligations. Multiple enacted laws across packaging, textiles, batteries, and gas cylinders now mandate minimum reuse performance or reward it with lower fees, making this lever both a compliance necessity and a cost-reduction opportunity.

Backed by 86 bills (84 enacted) — Required in 16 state(s) · Exemption available in 12 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 10 state(s) · Referenced in 4 state(s) · Fee-penalized in 1 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Engineer packaging to survive at least two full trips or rotations (required). Structural design, closures, and materials must withstand a minimum of two use cycles before end-of-life, as required for packaging classified as 'destined for reuse' under Irish packaging regulations. — IE eli/2007/si/798/made/en, IE eli/2014/si/282/made/en
  • Attach a deposit-return or equivalent take-back system to refillable cylinders (required). Gas cylinders and refillable bottles placed on market must be paired with a consigne (deposit) scheme or a functionally equivalent return system that incentivises consumer return and enables multiple refills across the product's lifecycle; free take-back must also be provided outside deposit channels. — FR cenv/L541-10-24, FR JORFTEXT000026863961, FR JORFTEXT000032770063, FR cenv/L541-10-22
  • Design transport packaging for reuse and track annual reuse rate (required). Pallet wrappings and straps must be designed for reuse and operators must achieve a 40% reuse rate annually; a 100% reuse requirement applies for most inter-company transports, with limited exemptions for intra-company and same-Member-State routes. — EU 32026D0429
  • Design textiles to be prepared for reuse and meet escalating annual weight targets (required). Textile products placed on the Dutch market must be designed to enable preparation for reuse, with operators meeting a minimum reuse weight percentage that rises from 20% in 2025 to 25% from 2030 onward, calculated against the prior year's market volume. — NL BWBR0048093, NL BWBR0048299
  • Qualify reusable/refillable packaging for EPR fee reductions through eco-modulation (fee-advantaged). Reusable and refillable packaging formats—including those meeting eco-organism standards—attract lower producer financial contributions under eco-modulated EPR schemes; batteries and tyres designed for reuse similarly receive fee advantages, so reuse capability should be documented and declared to PROs. — FR cenv/L541-10-3, MN HF-3911, DE battdg, ES BOE-A-2025-17186, NL BWBR0044197, EU 32018L0852
  • Design reusable packaging with a minimum four-year service life to spread recovery obligations (exemption). Packaging demonstrably designed for a service life of at least four years can qualify for exemptions from certain recovery obligations; pairing this with a deposit or direct-exchange arrangement can also exempt operators from PRO participation requirements in some jurisdictions. — UK uksi/1999/3447, AT 20008902

Targets to hit

  • 40% annual reuse rate for pallet wrappings and straps (EU 32026D0429)
  • Minimum 2 trips or rotations for reusable packaging (IE eli/2007/si/798/made/en, IE eli/2014/si/282/made/en)
  • 20% textile weight prepared for reuse in 2025, rising to 25% from 2030 (NL BWBR0048093)
  • At least 50% of a 20% single-use plastic packaging tonnage reduction achieved via reuse/reusable systems by 31 December 2025 (FR JORFTEXT000043458675)
  • Minimum 4-year service life for reusable packaging qualifying for recovery-obligation spread (UK uksi/1999/3447)
  • Reusable plastic bags must have film thickness ≥50 micrometers and be labeled as such (JP 418M60000740001)
Evidence (statutory language)
"Economic operators using pallet wrappings and straps are subject to the overall 40% re-use target under Article 29(1) of Regulation (EU) 2025/40 for transport packaging formats in a calendar year (not exempted by this Decision)"
— EU 32026D0429 (enacted, Required)
"Economic operators are exempted from the 100% re-use requirement for intra-company or linked/partner company transports within the EU (Article 29(2) of Regulation (EU) 2025/40) specifically for pallet wrappings and straps"
— EU 32026D0429 (enacted, Exemption available)
"Economic operators are exempted from the 100% re-use requirement for transports between companies within the same Member State (Article 29(3) of Regulation (EU) 2025/40) specifically for pallet wrappings and straps"
— EU 32026D0429 (enacted, Exemption available)

Eliminate restricted substances (PFAS, heavy metals, etc.)

Toxics elimination requires design teams to remove or reduce restricted substances—PFAS, heavy metals (Pb, Cd, Hg, Cr⁶⁺), and brominated flame retardants—from products, packaging, and components before market entry. Multiple enacted laws set hard concentration ceilings across electronics, batteries, packaging, vehicles, and textiles, making proactive material substitution a legal baseline, not a stretch goal.

Backed by 201 bills (197 enacted) — Required in 26 state(s) · Exemption available in 14 state(s) · Referenced in 8 state(s) · Prohibited in 6 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 6 state(s) · Fee-penalized in 3 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Strip lead, cadmium, mercury, and Cr⁶⁺ from all packaging materials (required). The aggregate concentration of Pb, Cd, Hg, and hexavalent chromium in any packaging or packaging component must not exceed 100 ppm (mg/kg) by weight; apply this ceiling to primary, secondary, and tertiary packaging alike. — NL BWBR0018139, PL DU/2019/542, PL DU/2024/927, EE 113032019103, DK lta/2025/1146, AT 20008902
  • Remove RoHS-restricted substances from all electrical and electronic equipment (required). Ensure no homogeneous material in EEE contains lead, mercury, or hexavalent chromium above 0.1 wt%, cadmium above 0.01 wt%, or any PBB/PBDE; track substance type and content rate at the part/component level as required for electronics placed on the market. — EU 32011L0065, EU 32002D0525, JP 413M60000400086, JP 413M60000400083
  • Keep mercury in all batteries below 0.0005 wt% and cadmium in portable batteries below 0.002 wt% (prohibited). These ceilings apply regardless of whether the battery is standalone or built into a device; design battery chemistries and select cell suppliers to meet both thresholds simultaneously, and verify compliance for every new battery SKU. — EU 32006L0066, AT 20005815, ES BOE-A-2008-2387, NL BWBR0024492
  • Eliminate all PFAS from carpet products and treatments (prohibited). No carpet sold or offered for sale may contain or be treated with any PFAS chemical; audit fiber, backing, adhesive, and stain-treatment inputs and substitute with PFAS-free alternatives before the statutory compliance date. — NY S-5027C
  • Remove lead from solders, PCB finishes, and termination finishes in automotive electronics (required). Lead must be fully eliminated from solders, termination finishes, and printed-circuit-board finishes used in ignition modules and other engine control systems; select lead-free solder alloys and surface finishes at the component specification stage. — EU 32014L0072
  • Verify hazardous-substance compliance across vehicle material supply chains (required). Establish contractual arrangements with Tier 1 and Tier 2 suppliers to confirm that vehicle materials comply with restrictions on hazardous substances, and maintain documented evidence of compliance for each material and part. — EU 32009L0001

Targets to hit

  • ≤0.0005 wt% mercury in all batteries (EU 32006L0066, AT 20005815, ES BOE-A-2008-2387, NL BWBR0024492)
  • ≤0.002 wt% cadmium in portable batteries (EU 32006L0066, AT 20005815, ES BOE-A-2008-2387, NL BWBR0024492)
  • ≤100 ppm (mg/kg) aggregate Pb + Cd + Hg + Cr⁶⁺ in packaging (NL BWBR0018139, PL DU/2019/542, PL DU/2024/927, EE 113032019103, DK lta/2025/1146, AT 20008902)
  • ≤0.1 wt% lead, mercury, or hexavalent chromium per homogeneous material in EEE (EU 32002D0525)
  • ≤0.01 wt% cadmium per homogeneous material in EEE (EU 32002D0525)
  • 0% PFAS in carpet composition and treatments (NY S-5027C)
  • 0% lead in solders and PCB finishes in automotive ignition/engine-control electronics (EU 32014L0072)
Evidence (statutory language)
"do not place on market batteries containing more than 0.0005 wt% mercury (regardless of whether built into devices)"
— AT 20005815 (enacted, Required)
"device batteries containing more than 0.002 wt% cadmium (including those built into devices)"
— AT 20005815 (enacted, Required)
"Prohibition on placing on the market any batteries or accumulators (incorporated or not into appliances) containing more than 0.0005% mercury by weight"
— ES BOE-A-2008-2387 (enacted, Prohibited)

Avoid banned / restricted materials and formats

Material restriction requires design teams to eliminate banned materials and formats—such as expanded polystyrene, oxo-degradable plastics, and non-compliant single-use plastics—before a product reaches market. Acting early avoids regulatory non-compliance, stranded tooling investment, and supply-chain disruption as these prohibitions expand across jurisdictions.

Backed by 98 bills (96 enacted) — Prohibited in 18 state(s) · Exemption available in 11 state(s) · Required in 8 state(s) · Fee-penalized in 8 state(s) · Referenced in 6 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Eliminate oxo-degradable plastic from all products and packaging immediately (prohibited). Oxo-degradable plastic is outright prohibited in both Germany and Austria; no product category or format is exempt, so remove it from all material specifications and supplier approved-material lists. — DE ewkverbotsv, AT 20002086
  • Remove expanded polystyrene from all food and beverage container designs (prohibited). EPS is prohibited for single-use food containers, beverage containers, and beverage cups under both UK Scottish and German law; substitute with paper-based, PET, steel, or aluminium formats depending on the application. — UK ssi/2021/410, DE ewkverbotsv
  • Eliminate plastic from single-use cutlery, plates, stirrers, straws, and balloon sticks (prohibited). Plastic is prohibited for these single-use formats across multiple enacted jurisdictions; redesign using unmodified natural polymers, paper, or other non-plastic materials, noting that unmodified natural polymers fall outside the statutory definition of 'plastic' and are therefore permissible. — UK ssi/2021/410, DE ewkverbotsv, DE ewkfondsg
  • Restrict beverage bottle plastic to PET only; attach caps permanently (required). Enacted law in Ireland and Scotland limits plastic beverage bottles to PET (excluding other plastic resins), while Austria, Poland, and Sweden require that plastic caps and lids remain physically attached to the container throughout its intended use phase. — IE eli/2024/si/33/made/en, UK ssi/2025/188, AT 20008902, PL DU/2023/1658, SE sfs-2018-1462
  • Replace lightweight plastic carrier bags with paper, cardboard, or bioplastic alternatives (prohibited). Austria prohibits plastic carrier bags outright; Latvia requires replacement of lightweight plastic shopping bags with paper, cardboard, or bioplastic by 2025-01-01; Poland prohibits carrier bags below 50 µm thickness—design teams should transition all retail bag formats to compliant alternatives, retaining very lightweight (<15 µm) film only where needed as primary hygiene or loose-food packaging. — AT 20002086, LV 57207, PL DU/2017/2056, SE sfs-2016-1041
  • Exclude tissue paper and wood-containing paper from EU Ecolabel scheme submissions (prohibited). Both tissue paper and all wood-containing paper grades are explicitly excluded from the EU Ecolabel paper scheme; product lines in these categories must not be submitted for certification under this scheme and should seek alternative compliance pathways. — EU 32008D0440

Targets to hit

  • Plastic carrier bags: wall thickness <15 µm qualifies as exempt very-lightweight primary packaging for hygiene or loose-weight food (SE sfs-2016-1041)
  • Plastic carrier bags: film thickness ≤49 µm is deemed single-use and subject to prohibition/charge; >49 µm is not deemed single-use (UK asc/2023/2)
  • Plastic carrier bags: thickness below 50 µm is prohibited for lightweight carrier bags (PL DU/2017/2056)
  • Beverage packaging: PET plastic, steel, or aluminium only, 150 ml–3 litres (UK ssi/2025/188)
  • Plastic shopping bags: ≥25% biomass-derived material by plastic weight (labeled) qualifies for charge exemption in Japan (JP 418M60000740001)
  • Plastic shopping bags: 100% marine-biodegradable plastic (labeled) qualifies for charge exemption in Japan (JP 418M60000740001)
  • Packaged products: maximum 90% by volume of meat-and-bone meal of Category 2 material (EU 32021R1929)
Evidence (statutory language)
"Do not manufacture single-use expanded polystyrene beverage containers, beverage cups, or food containers using expanded polystyrene."
— UK ssi/2021/410 (enacted, Prohibited)
"Do not manufacture single-use plastic beverage stirrers, cutlery, or plates using plastic."
— UK ssi/2021/410 (enacted, Prohibited)
"Do not supply, offer to supply, or possess for supply (in the course of a business) single-use plastic straws to an end-user, except where a statutory exemption applies."
— UK ssi/2021/410 (enacted, Prohibited)

Use certified-compostable materials where specified

Compostability as a design lever requires that materials certified for composting genuinely meet end-of-life processing conditions—home, industrial, or on-site—and are not mislabeled. Multiple enacted laws across the US and EU mandate separate collection, certified compostable outputs, and accurate labeling, making material selection and claims a legal compliance issue, not just a sustainability aspiration.

Backed by 18 bills (17 enacted) — Required in 7 state(s) · Exemption available in 3 state(s) · Prohibited in 1 state(s) · Referenced in 1 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 1 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Never label industrial-only compostable packaging as 'compostable' without qualification (prohibited). If a material requires industrial composting infrastructure to break down, it must not carry an unqualified 'compostable' claim; labeling must accurately reflect the required processing environment. — FR JORFTEXT000041553759
  • Use home-compostable, bio-based materials for direct fruit and vegetable labels (required). Labels applied directly to fresh produce must be both home-compostable and made wholly or partly of bio-based materials to comply with enacted law. — FR JORFTEXT000042753962
  • Ensure compostable packaging outputs are reprocessed into a usable soil conditioner, fertiliser, or growing medium (required). Compostable or biodegradable packaging waste processed via aerobic or anaerobic treatment must yield a product usable as a soil conditioner, fertiliser, or growing medium to count toward recycling targets. — UK wsi/2011/551, EU 32018L0852
  • Design organic waste packaging to support separate collection and composting infrastructure (required). Biodegradable and organic waste packaging must be designed to enable separate collection and must not be mixed with non-sorted waste streams, in support of landfill diversion and composting rate targets. — FR JORFTEXT000032187830, EU 32018L0850, WA SB-5284, CA AB-1857
  • Design food-waste packaging to be compatible with dewatering, drying, fermentation, or carbonization processing (required). For food-sector packaging, material choices should support thermal recovery methods including dewatering, drying, fermentation, and carbonization as defined under Japanese food recycling regulations. — JP 413M60001200002
  • Design on-site compostable biodegradable materials to qualify for waste-prevention or fee-exemption status (exemption). Where biodegradable materials are composted by the producer for own use or transferred in accordance with fertilizer law without environmental harm, they may be classified as waste prevention; residential on-site composting declarations can also unlock partial fee exemptions. — CZ 2020/541, PL DU/2021/1648

Targets to hit

  • 75% reduction in organic waste from 2014 levels by 2025 (CA AB-1857)
  • On-site composting feedstock not exceeding 200 cubic yards at any one time to qualify for permit exemption (CA SB-279)
Evidence (statutory language)
"Prohibition: plastic products and packaging whose compostability can only be achieved in industrial composting units may NOT bear the label 'compostable' (Art. 13)"
— FR JORFTEXT000041553759 (enacted, Prohibited)
"Prohibition on applying labels directly to fruits and vegetables unless the labels are home-compostable and made wholly or partly of bio-based materials"
— FR JORFTEXT000042753962 (enacted, Required)

— CA SB-279 (enacted, Exemption available)
Part 2 — Product design & right to repair

Adjacent legislation: right-to-repair and durability laws don't use EPR fees, but they impose design obligations on the same product teams — and they are the fastest-moving category in this dataset.

Design for repairability, spare-parts access, and longevity

Design for repairability, spare-parts access, and longevity requires that products be built to last, use standardized and interchangeable parts, and have critical spare parts available from day one of market launch through at least five years after the last unit is sold. Acting now positions design teams ahead of enacted ecodesign mandates across electronics, furniture, batteries, and tyres while reducing warranty costs and enabling circular service models.

Backed by 115 bills (113 enacted) — Required in 18 state(s) · Referenced in 10 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 8 state(s) · Exemption available in 6 state(s) · Fee-penalized in 1 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Make critical spare parts available at market launch and continuously thereafter (required). For electronics (smartphones, laptops), stock high-wear parts—displays, batteries, cameras, chargers, storage drives—from the date the first unit goes on sale, and maintain availability of all listed spare parts for a minimum of five years after the last unit of that model is sold. — FR JORFTEXT000044806559, FR JORFTEXT000045072860, FR cenv/L541-10, EU 32011L0065
  • Standardize and commonize parts across product models to enable repair (required). For electronics, furniture, and appliances, use shared circuit boards, gas springs, connectors, and structural components across model lines so that a single spare part services multiple SKUs, reducing inventory burden and extending repairability across the portfolio. — JP 413M60000400063, JP 413M60000400068, JP 413M60000400071, JP 413M60000400074, JP 413M60000400073
  • Adopt high-durability materials and components in structural and wear-prone assemblies (required). Specify long-life compressors, drive units, seat surfaces, cabinet structures, and tabletop materials at the component-selection stage; prioritize parts prone to rapid deterioration (e.g., gaskets, bearings, upholstery) for easy user or technician replacement. — JP 413M60000400063, JP 413M60000400068, JP 413M60000400072, JP 413M60000400074, EU 32024R1781, FI 2021/714
  • Publish repair information and technical documentation to independent repairers (required). Within one year of first placing a new EEE type on the market, provide free-of-charge maintenance, repair, and disassembly information—including component locations, materials, and hazardous-substance data—to all professional and individual repairers, not only authorized service networks. — LU eli/etat/leg/loi/2022/06/09/a266/jo/fr, FI 2021/714, FR cenv/L541-10
  • Eliminate design features that intentionally impede repair, upgrade, or disassembly (required). Avoid adhesives, proprietary fasteners, or software locks that prevent battery removal, module replacement, or component-level repair unless a documented, overriding environmental or safety benefit justifies the constraint. — FI 2014/519, EU 32024R1781
  • Integrate durability and repairability into battery and tyre product design programs (required). For battery products, design for extended operational life to qualify for reduced PRO financial contributions under eco-modulation schemes; for tyre manufacturing, document and apply mechanisms that extend tyre service life and facilitate end-of-life reuse as part of a formal waste-prevention plan. — DE battdg, ES BOE-A-2025-17186

Targets to hit

  • Spare parts available from day 0 (market launch) for displays, batteries, cameras, chargers, and storage drives on smartphones and laptops (FR JORFTEXT000044806559)
  • Secondary spare parts (motherboards, RAM, fans, keyboards, charge ports) available within 2 years of market launch for laptops and smartphones (FR JORFTEXT000044806559)
  • All listed spare parts maintained for minimum 5 years after last unit sold — smartphones, laptops, and mobility-aid vehicles (FR JORFTEXT000044806559, FR JORFTEXT000045072860)
  • Repair information provided free of charge within 1 year of first market placement for new EEE types (LU eli/etat/leg/loi/2022/06/09/a266/jo/fr)

Methodology & limits
  • Source. 471 bills across 35 states classified and analyzed by Atlas Circular; design signals extracted from each bill's compliance text and verified to be verbatim before inclusion (no paraphrased citations).
  • Authority. Enacted laws are current obligations; proposed bills indicate direction and may change or fail. Each principle notes how many of its backing bills are enacted.
  • Coverage. Drawn from the bills with full compliance extractions to date — strong on flagship enacted programs (CA, OR, CO, ME, CT, MN, VT), lighter on early-stage bills.
  • Costs. No fee or cost figures are asserted; eco-modulation rates are set by producer responsibility organizations (e.g. Circular Action Alliance) and are pending. Once published, each design lever maps to fee exposure based on the volume of covered product you sell.
