Designing for Circularity: A Practitioner's Guide

SignalScout · Circularity Intelligence · Compiled 15 June 2026

How to design packaging and products that stay compliant — and pay lower fees — under US Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) and adjacent circular-economy law.

Derived from 90 bills across 17 states (54 already enacted) that our system has classified and analyzed. Every principle below traces to specific bills, with the exact statutory language quoted. Proposed bills show the direction of travel; enacted laws are obligations today.

How to use this guide. Each lever is a design decision you control. For every lever: the *imperatives* are what to do, *targets* are the hard numbers to hit, and *evidence* shows the laws driving it. Audit your SKUs lever by lever; prioritize the states you sell into. Fee figures are intentionally omitted until the Circular Action Alliance publishes eco-modulation rates — at which point design choices map directly to dollar exposure.

Part 1 — Packaging design

Design packaging to be recyclable in available systems

Design for recycling requires that packaging and products be compatible with existing collection, sorting, and end-market systems — not merely labeled as recyclable. Multiple enacted state laws now mandate or financially reward this compatibility, making it a compliance baseline rather than a voluntary aspiration.

Backed by 44 bills (24 enacted) — Required in 12 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 6 state(s) · Exemption available in 3 state(s) · Referenced in 1 state(s) · Fee-penalized in 1 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Ensure general packaging is recyclable in available collection systems (required). Packaging must be made of material types and forms that are routinely collected, sorted, and converted into feedstock by programs covering the population where the product is sold; in California this means coverage by programs serving at least 60% of the state's population before any recyclability claim or chasing-arrows symbol may be displayed. — CA SB-343, CA SB-54, CO HB-22-1355, WA SB-5284, ME LD-1423
  • Design batteries and electronics for end-of-life recyclability and easy removal (required). Batteries must be removable by the end user using no more than common household tools, and both batteries and covered electronic equipment must be compatible with certified collection and recycling programs; follow the waste-management hierarchy (prevention → reuse → recycling) before considering other end-of-life options. — CA AB-2440, WA SB-5144, DC D.C. Law 24-320, VT S-254
  • Design apparel and textiles to enable sorting and recycling processes (required). Apparel and textile articles sold in California must be designed to support downstream collection, sorting, and recycling operations as required by producer responsibility stewardship plans covering those end-of-life pathways. — CA SB-707
  • Design beverage containers in recyclable material types for certified systems (required). Beverage containers — including standard and miniature sizes in aluminum, bimetal, glass, and plastic — must be compatible with certified recycling center acceptance and refund-value programs in applicable states. — CA AB-1311, RI HB-6207, RI SB-996
  • Design solar panels and related infrastructure components for end-of-life recycling (required). Solar or photovoltaic energy generation facilities and all associated equipment and infrastructure must be designed so that they can be fully removed and recycled after termination of use, as required under New Jersey law. — NJ S-3399
  • Improve packaging recyclability to reduce eco-modulated fee exposure (fee-advantaged). In states with producer responsibility fee structures that apply eco-modulation factors — including recyclability — improving a package's recyclability profile can reduce fee obligations; prioritize material types on minimum recyclable lists developed under applicable programs. — ME LD-1541, MN HF-3911, CO HB22-1355

Targets to hit

  • 60% of California's population served by collection programs — required before displaying chasing-arrows symbol (CA SB-343)
  • 65% recycling rate for 3 consecutive years prior to January 1, 2027 — required to qualify for ME packaging exemption (ME LD-1423)
  • 70% recycling rate annually on and after January 1, 2027 — required to maintain ME packaging exemption (ME LD-1423)
  • 100% of covered packaging recyclable or compostable by January 1, 2032 (CA SB-54)
Evidence (statutory language)
"Ensure that any product or packaging displaying a chasing arrows symbol meets statewide recyclability criteria (collected for recycling by programs covering at least 60% of California's population) and is of a material type and form that routinely becomes feedstock used in production of new products or packaging"
— CA SB-343 (enacted, Required)
"Ensure covered material offered for sale, distributed, or imported in or into the state on or after January 1, 2032, is recyclable or compostable"
— CA SB-54 (enacted, Required)
"Batteries designed to be easily removed from a product by the user with no more than common household tools"
— CA AB-2440 (enacted, Required)

Incorporate post-consumer recycled content

Incorporating post-consumer recycled (PCR) content is now a hard legal requirement across multiple product categories in several enacted US state laws, with mandatory minimums ranging from 10% to 100% depending on material and sector. Design teams must embed PCR targets into material specifications early, as compliance timelines are already active or imminent and eco-modulated fee structures in EPR programs further reward higher recycled content.

Backed by 15 bills (9 enacted) — Fee-advantaged in 6 state(s) · Required in 3 state(s) · Exemption available in 2 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Set PCR content floors for plastic beverage containers by 2026 (required). Plastic beverage containers sold in Maine must average at least 25% post-consumer recycled plastic from January 1, 2026 through December 31, 2030, rising to 30% from 2031 onward; California imposes separate PCR requirements for filled plastic beverage containers under SB-1013. — ME LD-1467, CA SB-1013
  • Specify minimum 35% post-consumer recycled glass in glass beverage containers (required). California law requires glass container manufacturers to use at least 35% post-consumer (postfilled) glass when manufacturing glass food, drink, or beverage containers. — CA SB-38
  • Build a stepwise PCR ramp into carpet product specifications (required). Carpet sold in New York must meet escalating post-consumer content minimums: 10% at year two, 20% at year six, and 30% at year eleven after the law's effective date — design teams should plan material sourcing roadmaps now to hit each threshold. — NY S-5027C
  • Target 65% recycled content in plastic covered materials for California compliance (required). California SB-54 requires that plastic covered material meet a recycled content rate of not less than 65% on and after January 1, 2032; design teams should begin qualifying recycled resin suppliers well ahead of that date. — CA SB-54
  • Increase PCR content in packaging to reduce eco-modulated EPR fees (fee-advantaged). Enacted EPR programs in Minnesota and Maine explicitly eco-modulate producer fees based on post-consumer recycled content levels, meaning higher PCR content directly lowers fee obligations; similar incentive structures are advancing in Vermont and Oregon. — MN HF-3911, ME LD-1541, VT H-915, OR HB-3780
  • Use 100% recycled material in paper carryout bags to avoid single-use ban (exemption). Colorado's carryout bag law exempts recycled paper bags made from 100% recycled or post-consumer content (with basis weight greater than 30 pounds) from the single-use plastic bag prohibition, making full recycled content the design threshold for compliance. — CO HB-21-1162

Targets to hit

  • 25% PCR plastic in beverage containers 2026–2030 (ME LD-1467)
  • 30% PCR plastic in beverage containers from 2031 (ME LD-1467)
  • 10% PCR content in carpet at year 2 (NY S-5027C)
  • 20% PCR content in carpet at year 6 (NY S-5027C)
  • 30% PCR content in carpet at year 11 (NY S-5027C)
  • 35% post-consumer recycled glass in glass beverage containers (CA SB-38)
  • 65% recycled content in plastic covered material by January 1, 2032 (CA SB-54)
  • 100% recycled content in paper carryout bags for ban exemption (CO HB-21-1162)
Evidence (statutory language)
"Beginning January 1, 2026 through December 31, 2030: ensure that the total number of plastic beverage containers sold, offered for sale, or distributed for sale in Maine contain, on average and in the aggregate, at least 25% post-consumer recycled plastic"
— ME LD-1467 (enacted, Required)
"Beginning January 1, 2031: ensure that the total number of plastic beverage containers sold, offered for sale, or distributed for sale in Maine contain, on average and in the aggregate, at least 30% post-consumer recycled plastic"
— ME LD-1467 (enacted, Required)
"Ensure carpet sold in the state is manufactured with minimum 10% post-consumer content (effective two years after effective date)"
— NY S-5027C (enacted, Required)

Reduce packaging material per unit (lightweight, right-size)

Source reduction means designing packaging to use less material per unit—through lightweighting, right-sizing, or eliminating unnecessary layers—without sacrificing function or recyclability. Multiple enacted US laws directly require or financially reward this approach, making it both a compliance obligation and a cost-reduction lever for producers selling into covered states.

Backed by 12 bills (9 enacted) — Referenced in 4 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 3 state(s) · Exemption available in 2 state(s) · Required in 2 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Actively source-reduce single-use plastic packaging material per unit (required). CA SB-54 explicitly requires producers to 'source reduce plastic covered material,' making this a direct legal obligation for single-use plastic packaging sold in California. — CA SB-54
  • Minimize total packaging material to prevent negative environmental impacts (required). CO HB-22-1355 requires covered materials to be designed and managed to prevent or minimize negative environmental, social, economic, and health impacts, establishing source reduction as a design standard in Colorado. — CO HB-22-1355
  • Lightweight packaging per unit without compromising recyclability (fee-advantaged). MN HF-3911 explicitly names 'lightweighting (reducing packaging per unit without compromising recyclability)' as a recognized practice, and ME LD-1541 assesses producer fees by weight of packaging sold, so reducing material weight directly lowers fee liability in both states. — MN HF-3911, ME LD-1541
  • Right-size packaging across the full material lifecycle from design to end-of-life (required). WA SB-5284 references 'material management from design concept to end of life,' signaling that Washington expects source reduction to be embedded in the design process, not treated as an afterthought. — WA SB-5284
  • Reduce paper carryout bag basis weight to 30 pounds or less to avoid fee (exemption). CO HB-21-1162 excludes bags with a paper basis weight of 30 pounds or less from the definition of 'carryout bag,' providing a clear, numeric threshold that exempts lightweight paper bags from fee obligations in Colorado. — CO HB-21-1162
  • Incorporate source reduction into integrated waste management planning for solid waste (required). CA AB-1857 requires solid waste to be subject to source reduction and recycling elements of integrated waste management plans, meaning California producers must formally account for packaging reduction in their waste management documentation. — CA AB-1857

Targets to hit

  • Paper carryout bag basis weight ≤ 30 pounds to qualify for carryout-bag definition exemption (CO HB-21-1162)
Evidence (statutory language)
"Bags with paper basis weight of 30 pounds or less are excluded from the definition of 'carryout bag'"
— CO HB-21-1162 (enacted, Exemption available)
"Payments assessed and collected from producers based on the weight (tons) of packaging material sold, offered for sale or distributed for sale in or into Maine by each producer."
— ME LD-1541 (enacted, Fee-advantaged)
"lightweighting (reducing packaging per unit without compromising recyclability)"
— MN HF-3911 (enacted, Fee-advantaged)

Shift to reusable / refillable formats

The 'reuse_refill' lever directs design teams to engineer products and packaging for multiple use cycles—through washable, refillable, or refurbishable formats—rather than single-use disposal. Multiple enacted US laws now mandate or financially reward reuse systems across beverages, industrial cylinders, paint, and general packaging, making durable reuse design a compliance requirement in several sectors.

Backed by 10 bills (8 enacted) — Required in 4 state(s) · Exemption available in 2 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 1 state(s) · Referenced in 1 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Design glass beverage containers to withstand commercial washing and refill cycles (required). Beverage containers must be engineered for repeated wash-and-refill processing by a certified processor; refillable glass carbonated beverage containers with a permanently marked brand name and minimum 5¢ refund value also qualify for label-marking exemptions. — CA AB-962, ME LD-1909
  • Engineer gas and LPG cylinders explicitly for refill, refurbishment, and reuse cycles (required). Cylinder design must support collection, refilling, and residual-gas capture within a stewardship program; LPG containers designed to be refilled qualify for regulatory exemption, while propane/gas cylinder programs require participation in collection and reuse stewardship. — CT HB-5142, OR SB-582, NY A-8195
  • Integrate packaging into closed-loop reuse systems to minimize material fees (fee-advantaged). Packaging placed into certified reuse systems is charged the producer-responsibility fee only once at initial market entry, so designing for reuse loops directly reduces ongoing financial obligations under the program. — MN HF-3911
  • Build reusable carryout bags to meet minimum durability and capacity specifications (exemption). To qualify as 'reusable' and escape single-use bag fees, bags must be designed and manufactured for at least 125 uses, carry a minimum of 22 lbs over 175 feet, have stitched handles, and be made of cloth, fiber, fabric, or recycled material such as PET. — CO HB-21-1162
  • Develop postconsumer paint products and packaging to support reuse recovery programs (required). Paint product stewardship plans must include a recovery program that actively promotes reuse of postconsumer paint, so formulation, container design, and labeling should facilitate identification, collection, and reuse rather than disposal. — CA SB-1143
  • Ensure packaging materials are compatible with funded refill and reuse collection infrastructure (required). State-funded covered-material management programs explicitly include refill and reuse as covered services alongside recycling and composting, so packaging should be designed to flow into these systems without contamination or format incompatibility. — WA SB-5284

Targets to hit

  • Reusable carryout bags: minimum 125 uses, carry ≥22 lbs over 175 feet (CO HB-21-1162)
  • Refillable glass carbonated beverage containers: minimum 5¢ refund value permanently marked on container (ME LD-1909)
Evidence (statutory language)
"Reusable carryout bags (designed/manufactured for at least 125 uses, carry at least 22 lbs over 175 feet, stitched handles, made of cloth/fiber/fabric or recycled material such as PET) are excluded from 'single-use plastic carryout bag' definition"
— CO HB-21-1162 (enacted, Exemption available)
"Beverage manufacturers must have their reusable glass beverage containers processed by a certified processor for subsequent washing for refill and sale"
— CA AB-962 (enacted, Required)
"Stewardship plan must provide for refilling or recycling cylinders and capturing residual gases for reuse"
— CT HB-5142 (enacted, Required)

Eliminate restricted substances (PFAS, heavy metals, etc.)

Toxics elimination requires design teams to remove restricted substances—PFAS, mercury, free-liquid electrolytes, and other hazardous chemicals—from products and packaging before they reach market. Multiple enacted US laws already prohibit or penalize these substances, making proactive elimination essential to avoid sales bans, EPR exclusions, and eco-modulated fee penalties.

Backed by 20 bills (10 enacted) — Prohibited in 7 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 4 state(s) · Referenced in 4 state(s) · Required in 3 state(s) · Exemption available in 2 state(s) · Fee-penalized in 1 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Eliminate all PFAS from carpet fiber, backing, and surface treatments (prohibited). No carpet sold in New York may contain or be treated with PFAS chemicals; design teams must audit every component—yarn, backing, stain-resist coatings—and qualify PFAS-free alternatives before the two-year compliance window closes. — NY S-5027C
  • Remove intentionally added toxic substances from all packaging formats (fee-advantaged). Minnesota's enacted packaging EPR law rewards elimination of intentionally added toxic substances through eco-modulated fee structures, and proposed laws in Oregon and Vermont apply the same logic; design teams should audit inks, adhesives, coatings, and substrate additives against restricted-substance lists and substitute safer alternatives. — MN HF-3911, ME LD-1541, OR HB-3780, VT H-142
  • Redesign batteries to use immobilized electrolyte; eliminate free-liquid electrolyte (prohibited). Four enacted state laws (Maine, Kentucky, Washington, Vermont) prohibit covered batteries containing electrolyte as a free liquid from EPR programs or ban their sale outright; specify gel, solid-state, or absorbed-glass-mat chemistries to maintain program eligibility across all covered jurisdictions. — ME LD-474, KY SB-49, WA SB-5144, VT S-254
  • Eliminate mercury from all thermostat and battery designs (prohibited). California enacted law prohibiting mercury-added thermostats, and proposed Minnesota bills would prohibit mercury in batteries above allowable thresholds; design teams should specify mercury-free switching mechanisms (e.g., electronic or bimetallic) and mercury-free electrochemical cells to achieve compliance across both product categories. — CA AB-732, CA AB-707, MN HF-4565, MN SF-4679
  • Remove PFAS from artificial turf infill, fibers, and binder components (required). New York's passed-chamber bill amends state environmental law to restrict PFAS in artificial turf; design teams specifying synthetic turf systems should qualify PFAS-free fiber and infill materials now to be ready for enactment. — NY S-10168
  • Optimize battery chemistry to minimize hazardous content and eco-modulated fees (fee-advantaged). Proposed EPR bills in Minnesota and Maryland indicate that battery fee structures will be eco-modulated based on hazardous content and recyclability; selecting lower-toxicity chemistries (e.g., lithium iron phosphate over cobalt-heavy formulations) positions products for lower fee tiers as these programs are finalized. — MN HF-4565, MN SF-4679, MD SB-686

Targets to hit

  • Sealed lead-acid battery packs must weigh ≤11 lbs and contain no liquid electrolyte to qualify for Vermont EPR stewardship exemption (VT S-254)
Evidence (statutory language)
"Ensure no carpet sold in the state contains or is treated with PFAS chemicals (effective two years after effective date)"
— NY S-5027C (enacted, Prohibited)
"mercury-added thermostats"
— CA AB-732 (enacted, Required)
"Batteries that contain an electrolyte as a free liquid"
— ME LD-474 (enacted, Prohibited)

Avoid banned / restricted materials and formats

Material restriction requires design teams to eliminate banned materials and formats before products reach market, avoiding regulatory non-compliance, disposal liability, and eco-modulated fee penalties. Enacted bans on specific plastics, foams, and film formats are already in force across multiple states, making proactive material substitution a near-term business necessity.

Backed by 15 bills (10 enacted) — Prohibited in 6 state(s) · Exemption available in 3 state(s) · Referenced in 3 state(s) · Required in 2 state(s) · Fee-penalized in 1 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Eliminate expanded polystyrene (EPS/Styrofoam) from all product and packaging formats (prohibited). Colorado prohibits manufacture and distribution of EPS products including blown polystyrene and expanded or extruded foam made from thermoplastic petrochemical styrene monomer; remove EPS from trays, clamshells, cups, and protective packaging. — CO HB-21-1162
  • Remove single-use plastic carryout bags from all retail packaging and fulfillment formats (prohibited). Colorado enacted a ban effective January 1, 2024, and Vermont has a parallel proposal in committee; design teams should treat single-use plastic bags as a prohibited format across all markets. — CO HB-21-1162, VT H-142
  • Replace noncompostable film plastic in direct-contact raw-meat packaging with compostable alternatives (prohibited). Washington prohibits noncompostable film plastic packaging used in direct contact with raw meat; food packaging designers must specify certified-compostable film for fresh and raw-meat applications. — WA SB-5284
  • Specify only materials on the approved recyclable list for covered products sold in Colorado (required). CO HB22-1355 requires that from July 1, 2025, covered products may only use materials that qualify under the state's producer responsibility program; audit material specs against the approved list before launch. — CO HB22-1355
  • Exclude fiberglass-containing components from children's products, mattresses, and upholstered furniture repairs (prohibited). New York's proposed bill prohibits custom upholsterers from using any replacement component containing fiberglass in children's products, mattresses, or upholstered/reupholstered furniture; furniture and textile designers should vet all fill and batting materials for fiberglass content. — NY A-7912
  • Avoid unsafe or unsanitary paper grades not accepted by recycling or composting facilities (prohibited). Minnesota references unsafe or unsanitary paper products not accepted by recycling or composting facilities as a restricted material category; specify only facility-accepted paper grades to maintain end-of-life pathway integrity. — MN HF-3911

Targets to hit

  • Single-use plastic carryout bags eliminated by January 1, 2024 (CO HB-21-1162)
  • Packaging materials intended for long-term storage must protect a durable product for at least 5 years to qualify for producer responsibility exemption (CO HB-22-1355)
Evidence (statutory language)
"On and after January 1, 2024: Do NOT provide single-use plastic carryout bags to customers (with limited inventory exception through June 1, 2024)"
— CO HB-21-1162 (enacted, Prohibited)
"Materials from a large-scale biomass management event must not include whole or partial animal carcasses or animal byproducts other than manure"
— CA SB-279 (enacted, Prohibited)
"Expanded polystyrene (EPS) products including blown polystyrene (Styrofoam), expanded or extruded foam made from thermoplastic petrochemical materials utilizing styrene monomer"
— CO HB-21-1162 (enacted, Prohibited)

Apply required recyclability / disposal labeling

Labeling and marking requirements span batteries, beverage containers, mattresses, home furnishings, and specialty materials, mandating that products carry chemistry identification, refund values, recyclability status, and disposal instructions directly on the product or packaging. Design teams must audit every product category against enacted state laws to avoid prohibited misleading claims and ensure compliant marks are embedded in packaging and product tooling before market launch.

Backed by 27 bills (13 enacted) — Required in 13 state(s) · Exemption available in 2 state(s) · Referenced in 2 state(s) · Prohibited in 1 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Mark covered batteries with chemistry type, producer ID, and disposal warning (required). All covered batteries (including those embedded in products) must be visibly marked with the manufacturer or producer name/brand, battery chemistry, and an explicit indication that the battery should not be disposed of as household waste — required in California (effective Jan 1, 2026), Kentucky, Connecticut, and DC. — CA SB-1215, KY SB-49, CT HB-5019, DC D.C. Law 24-320
  • Never place chasing-arrows resin code unless product meets recyclability standards (prohibited). On rigid plastic bottles and containers sold in California, the resin identification code may only appear inside chasing arrows if the product demonstrably meets statewide recyclability requirements; remove or correct any deceptive recyclability claim, symbol, or direction that does not meet this standard. — CA SB-343
  • Emboss or permanently mark refund value and state identifier on beverage containers (required). Refundable beverage containers sold in Maine must clearly show the refund value (or 'RV') and 'Maine'/'ME' via embossing, stamping, labeling, or secure attachment — not on the container bottom; Oregon requires a refund value statement on the label; California requires labeling per the Beverage Container Recycling and Litter Reduction Act; Michigan (proposed) requires the Michigan deposit value mark. — ME LD-1909, OR SB-1520, CA SB-1013, CA AB-1311
  • Include flame retardant chemical statement on home furnishing and insulation labels (required). Manufacturers of home furnishings and thermal insulation sold in California must include a flame retardant chemical statement directly on the product label, ensuring consumers can identify chemical content at point of use. — CA SB-814
  • Label mattresses with recycling information and free drop-off or collection options (required). Mattress manufacturers or stewardship program operators in California must educate consumers — via product labeling or accompanying materials — about how to recycle used mattresses and where free drop-off or collection is available. — CA AB-1478
  • Affix permanent fiberglass warning label on children's products, mattresses, and upholstered furniture (required). Any new children's product, mattress, or upholstered furniture containing fiberglass sold in New York must carry a prominent, permanent label warning that the product contains fiberglass which can be harmful if ingested, inhaled, or exposed to skin, visible before and during use. — NY A-7912

Targets to hit

  • Refund value ≥5¢ permanently marked on refillable glass carbonated containers exempts them from standard label marking (ME LD-1909)
  • Newly covered California beverage containers exempt from labeling until July 1, 2025 (CA SB-1013)
  • Battery-embedded product labels required for products sold on or after January 1, 2026 (CA SB-1215)

Use certified-compostable materials where specified

Compostability is an enacted alternative pathway to recyclability for covered packaging and organic-waste materials in California and Washington, with hard deadlines and sector-specific exemptions already in law. Design teams must act now to audit material portfolios, pursue third-party certification, and align with state-specific targets before 2025–2032 compliance windows close.

Backed by 5 bills (5 enacted) — Required in 2 state(s) · Exemption available in 1 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Design covered packaging to be compostable as recyclability alternative by 2032 (required). All covered material offered for sale, distributed, or imported into California on or after January 1, 2032, must be either recyclable or compostable — compostability is a fully valid compliance path under CA SB-54. — CA SB-54
  • Design organic-waste packaging for compostability to support 75% disposal reduction (required). Packaging associated with organic waste must be designed for compostability to contribute to California's target of a 75% reduction in statewide organic-waste disposal from 2014 levels by 2025. — CA AB-1857
  • Design packaging to meet composting rate targets in Washington-approved plans (required). Packaging sold or distributed in Washington must be designed to support composting collection and rate targets as established in producer-responsibility plans approved under WA SB-5284. — WA SB-5284
  • Label carpet 'compostable' per CA Section 42357 to secure product exemption (exemption). Carpet products that meet the compostability requirements of California Public Resources Code Section 42357 and carry the corresponding label are exempt from covered-product obligations under CA AB-863 — pursue certification and labeling to unlock this exemption. — CA AB-863
  • Use only qualifying organic feedstocks for permit-free on-site composting operations (exemption). Composting operations using exclusively green, agricultural, food, or vegetative materials with total feedstock and finished compost not exceeding 200 cubic yards on-site at any one time qualify as an excluded activity requiring no permit under CA SB-279. — CA SB-279

Part 2 — Product design & right to repair

Adjacent legislation: right-to-repair and durability laws don't use EPR fees, but they impose design obligations on the same product teams — and they are the fastest-moving category in this dataset.

Design for repairability, spare-parts access, and longevity

Design for repairability requires that products be engineered so that owners and independent repair providers can diagnose, maintain, and fix them using accessible documentation, parts, software, and tools — on terms no worse than those offered to authorized repair networks. Multiple enacted US state laws now mandate these obligations across electronics, powered wheelchairs, agricultural/digital equipment, and motor vehicles, making repair-access a baseline compliance requirement rather than a differentiator.

Backed by 32 bills (21 enacted) — Required in 8 state(s) · Exemption available in 8 state(s) · Prohibited in 3 state(s) · Referenced in 2 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 1 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Publish and freely distribute full repair documentation at product launch (required). Make manuals, schematics, service code descriptions, and diagnostic guides available at no charge to owners and independent repair providers; physical printed copies may recover only reasonable actual preparation and shipping costs. — NY S-4104, CA SB-244, RI HB-5017, RI SB-884, CA SB-1384
  • Supply parts, firmware, and software tools on most-favorable-terms parity (required). Price all parts, embedded software, firmware, and non-software tools at no greater than manufacturer's suggested retail price and on terms — including discounts, rebates, and delivery conditions — at least as favorable as those offered to any authorized repair provider; do not gate access on entering an authorized-repair arrangement. — NY S-4104, CO HB-22-1031, RI SB-884, RI HB-5017, CA SB-1384
  • Provide software diagnostic tools without authorization barriers or internet dependencies (required). Software tools used for diagnosis, maintenance, calibration, or part pairing must be delivered at no charge, must not require manufacturer authorization or a live internet connection to function, and must enable full repair functionality without imposed impediments. — NY S-4104, CO HB-22-1031, RI SB-884
  • Design electronic security locks with serviceable, resettable architectures (required). Any product containing an electronic security lock or security-related function must ship with — or provide access to — the documentation, firmware, and tools needed to reset that lock after it is disabled during servicing; secure release systems are permissible as the delivery mechanism. — CA SB-1384, CO HB-22-1031
  • Integrate nonproprietary diagnostic interfaces in motor vehicles and connected equipment (required). Motor vehicles must expose on-board diagnostic and repair data through an off-the-shelf PC interface compliant with SAE J2534, SAE J1939, or ISO 22900, and must incorporate an interoperable, owner-authorization access platform standardized across all makes and models by 2027. — ME LD-2211
  • Maintain stocked spare-parts availability for powered wheelchairs with rapid-order commitments (required). Wheelchair dealers must order required repair parts within 3 business days of assessing the need (or receiving insurer authorization) and complete repairs within 10 business days of the repair request; at minimum, batteries, chargers, joysticks, wheel assemblies, armrests, casters, and antitip devices must be kept available — noting that programmable positioning components requiring clinical calibration are exempt from independent-repair-access requirements. — CT SB-308, CA SB-1384

Targets to hit

  • 3-year repair documentation, parts, and tools availability for products with wholesale price $50–$99.99 (CA SB-244)
  • 7-year repair documentation, parts, and tools availability for products with wholesale price $100 or more (CA SB-244)
  • 7-year all-inclusive warranty for carpet sold in New York (NY S-5027C)
  • 10 business days maximum turnaround for wheelchair repairs in Connecticut (CT SB-308)

Methodology & limits
  • Source. 90 bills across 17 states classified and analyzed by SignalScout; design signals extracted from each bill's compliance text and verified to be verbatim before inclusion (no paraphrased citations).
  • Authority. Enacted laws are current obligations; proposed bills indicate direction and may change or fail. Each principle notes how many of its backing bills are enacted.
  • Coverage. Drawn from the bills with full compliance extractions to date — strong on flagship enacted programs (CA, OR, CO, ME, CT, MN, VT), lighter on early-stage bills.
  • Costs. No fee or cost figures are asserted; eco-modulation rates are set by producer responsibility organizations (e.g. Circular Action Alliance) and are pending. Once published, each design lever maps to fee exposure based on the volume of covered product you sell.
