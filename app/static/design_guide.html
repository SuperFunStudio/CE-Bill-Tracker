Designing for Circularity: A Practitioner's Guide

SignalScout · Circularity Intelligence · Compiled 23 July 2026

How to design packaging and products that stay compliant — and pay lower fees — under US Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) and adjacent circular-economy law.

Derived from 605 bills across 46 states (567 already enacted) that our system has classified and analyzed. Every principle below traces to specific bills, with the exact statutory language quoted. Proposed bills show the direction of travel; enacted laws are obligations today.

How to use this guide. Each lever is a design decision you control. For every lever: the *imperatives* are what to do, *targets* are the hard numbers to hit, and *evidence* shows the laws driving it. Audit your SKUs lever by lever; prioritize the states you sell into. Fee figures are intentionally omitted until the Circular Action Alliance publishes eco-modulation rates — at which point design choices map directly to dollar exposure.

Part 1 — Packaging design

Design packaging to be recyclable in available systems

Design for recycling requires that packaging and products be engineered to move through available collection, sorting, and reprocessing systems rather than ending up in landfill or incineration. Multiple enacted laws across the US, EU, and key trading-partner jurisdictions impose mandatory recyclability thresholds and design obligations, making this lever both a compliance necessity and a market-access requirement.

Backed by 371 bills (344 enacted) — Required in 41 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 18 state(s) · Referenced in 14 state(s) · Exemption available in 9 state(s) · Fee-penalized in 3 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Design all packaging to be recyclable in available collection systems (required). Packaging must be recyclable (or compostable) in systems that actually operate at scale; in California, a product may not display a chasing-arrows symbol unless it is collected for recycling by programs covering at least 60% of the state's population and routinely becomes feedstock for new products or packaging. — CA SB-54, CA SB-343, PL DU/2024/927, PL DU/2019/542
  • Eliminate materials and additives that disrupt downstream recycling streams (required). Prioritize non-toxic, non-hazardous, easily disassembled, and easily recyclable materials in all product and packaging design; avoid components that contaminate or degrade recycling feedstock quality. — CN 4028abcc61277793016127e112bb1ff4, CN ff80808175265dd40176b843949f3d0c, CN 4028abcc61277793016127c954530a34, FR JORFTEXT000032847905
  • Design plastic packaging to be fully recyclable with an operational recycling stream (required). All plastic packaging must have an operational recycling stream in place; France's enacted law targets 100% recyclability of single-use plastic packaging, and Austria's enacted law prohibits placing non-reusable, non-recyclable plastic packaging on the market from 1 January 2030. — FR JORFTEXT000043458675, AT 20008902
  • Design electronics and electrical equipment to meet category-specific recovery and recycling targets (required). EEE must be designed so that end-of-life units achieve the statutory recovery and reuse/recycling rates for their equipment category (e.g., ≥80% recovery and ≥75% reuse/recycling for large household appliances; ≥70% recovery and ≥50% reuse/recycling for IT and consumer equipment); design for disassembly is the primary enabler. — NL BWBR0017053, CA AB-2440
  • Design batteries to enable material recovery at statutory minimum rates (required). Battery design must allow recovery of lithium, cobalt, and other recoverable materials; recoverable material must constitute at least 30% of total lithium battery weight, and design must permit user removal with common household tools to facilitate collection and recycling. — JP 508M60001440001, JP 508M60001400002, JP 413M60001500001, CA AB-2440
  • Design textiles and vehicles to meet sector-specific recyclability and recovery targets (required). Textile products must be designed so that at least 50% by weight (rising to 75% by 2030) is prepared for reuse or recycled each year; vehicles must be designed to achieve at least 85% reuse/recyclability and 95% reuse/recoverability by mass, verified through type-approval. — NL BWBR0048093, EU 32000L0053, EU 32005L0064, SE sfs-2023-132

Targets to hit

  • 60% of California's population covered by recycling programs before chasing-arrows symbol may be used (CA SB-343)
  • 100% recyclability of single-use plastic packaging with operational recycling stream by 1 January 2025 (FR JORFTEXT000043458675)
  • 100% of plastic packaging reusable or recyclable from 1 January 2030 (AT 20008902)
  • Covered material recyclable or compostable by 1 January 2032 (CA SB-54)
  • Vehicles: ≥85% reusable/recyclable by mass, ≥95% reusable/recoverable by mass (EU 32000L0053, EU 32005L0064, SE sfs-2023-132)
  • Vehicles: ≥85% reuse/material recycling, ≥95% reuse/recovery by weight from 2015 (DE altautov, SE sfs-2007-185)
  • EEE large household appliances: ≥80% recovery, ≥75% reuse/recycling per year (NL BWBR0017053)
  • EEE IT/consumer equipment: ≥70% recovery, ≥50% reuse/recycling per year (NL BWBR0017053)
  • Textiles: 50% reuse-or-recycle in 2025, rising to 75% from 2030 onwards (NL BWBR0048093)
  • Lithium batteries: recoverable materials ≥30% of total battery weight (JP 508M60001440001, JP 508M60001400002)
  • Lithium battery recycling rate target ≥30% by weight of recoverable materials (JP 413M60001500001)
  • Sealed nickel-cadmium battery recycling rate ≥60% by weight (JP 413M60001500001)
  • Sealed nickel-metal hydride battery recycling rate ≥55% by weight (JP 413M60001500001)
  • Sealed lead-acid battery recycling rate ≥50% by weight (JP 413M60001500001)
  • Tyres: ≥20% material recycling by weight from 1 January 2005 (NL BWBR0016459)

Incorporate post-consumer recycled content

Incorporating post-consumer recycled (PCR) content is the single most legislated design lever in circular-economy law, with enacted mandates spanning plastic beverage bottles, glass containers, carpet, and paper bags across multiple US states and international jurisdictions. Design teams that embed PCR targets early in material specification will avoid compliance gaps as stepped thresholds tighten through 2032.

Backed by 96 bills (88 enacted) — Required in 17 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 13 state(s) · Referenced in 6 state(s) · Exemption available in 3 state(s) · Prohibited in 1 state(s) · Fee-penalized in 1 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Design plastic beverage bottles to meet stepped PCR content thresholds (required). For plastic beverage containers sold in Maine, specify at least 25% PCR plastic by 2026 and 30% by 2031, averaged across your full container portfolio; similar 25%/30% step-up schedules are enacted in multiple jurisdictions, making this the baseline global design standard for plastic beverage packaging. — ME LD-1467, CA SB-1013
  • Specify minimum 35% post-consumer recycled glass in beverage and food containers (required). California law requires glass container manufacturers to use at least 35% post-filled (recycled) glass in food, drink, or beverage containers, so glass packaging specifications must include verified PCR glass sourcing at or above this level for California market entry. — CA SB-38
  • Engineer plastic covered material to achieve 65% recycled content by 2032 (required). California SB-54 requires that plastic covered material meet a recycled content rate of not less than 65% on and after January 1, 2032; design teams should begin material qualification and supplier development now to hit this high threshold. — CA SB-54
  • Design carpet products with a rising PCR content schedule reaching 30% (required). New York's enacted carpet stewardship law mandates 10% PCR content two years after the effective date, rising to 20% at six years and 30% at eleven years; carpet designers must build a material roadmap that phases in increasing recycled fiber content across this timeline. — NY S-5027C
  • Use 100% recycled or post-consumer content in paper carryout bags (exemption). Colorado's enacted law defines a qualifying 'recycled paper carryout bag' as one made from 100% recycled material or post-consumer content with a basis weight greater than 30 pounds, which is the threshold needed to access the exemption from the single-use plastic bag restriction. — CO HB-21-1162
  • Set and track recycled plastic usage targets for electronics and appliances (required). Enacted Japanese regulations require manufacturers of unit-type air conditioners and electric refrigerators to establish planned improvement targets for recycled plastic usage volume and rate, and to increase the ratio of used rigid PVC pipes/fittings in pipe manufacturing to the extent technically and economically feasible; design teams in these product categories should integrate recycled plastic targets into annual product planning cycles. — JP 508M60000400008, JP 508M60000400010, JP 413M60000400059

Targets to hit

  • 25% PCR plastic in plastic beverage containers by 2026 (ME LD-1467)
  • 30% PCR plastic in plastic beverage containers by 2031 (ME LD-1467)
  • 10% PCR content in carpet, 2 years after effective date (NY S-5027C)
  • 20% PCR content in carpet, 6 years after effective date (NY S-5027C)
  • 30% PCR content in carpet, 11 years after effective date (NY S-5027C)
  • 35% post-consumer recycled glass in glass beverage/food containers (CA SB-38)
  • 65% recycled content in plastic covered material by January 1, 2032 (CA SB-54)
  • 100% recycled/post-consumer content in qualifying paper carryout bags (CO HB-21-1162)
  • 25% recycled plastic in PET single-use beverage bottles ≤3L by 2025 (PL DU/2024/927, PL DU/2023/1658)
  • 30% recycled plastic in all single-use plastic beverage bottles ≤3L by 2030 (PL DU/2023/1658)
Evidence (statutory language)
"Beginning January 1, 2026 through December 31, 2030: ensure that the total number of plastic beverage containers sold, offered for sale, or distributed for sale in Maine contain, on average and in the aggregate, at least 25% post-consumer recycled plastic"
— ME LD-1467 (enacted, Required)
"Beginning January 1, 2031: ensure that the total number of plastic beverage containers sold, offered for sale, or distributed for sale in Maine contain, on average and in the aggregate, at least 30% post-consumer recycled plastic"
— ME LD-1467 (enacted, Required)
"Ensure carpet sold in the state is manufactured with minimum 10% post-consumer content (effective two years after effective date)"
— NY S-5027C (enacted, Required)

Reduce packaging material per unit (lightweight, right-size)

Source reduction requires designing packaging and product components to use the minimum material necessary — eliminating unnecessary packaging and lightweighting what remains. Multiple enacted laws across the EU, Japan, France, Finland, Estonia, and China mandate minimization of packaging volume and weight, making this lever a compliance baseline, not merely a best practice.

Backed by 182 bills (179 enacted) — Required in 26 state(s) · Exemption available in 13 state(s) · Fee-penalized in 12 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 9 state(s) · Referenced in 8 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Minimize packaging volume and weight at the unit design stage (required). Packaging must be designed to the smallest dimensions and lowest mass that still meet safety, hygiene, transport, and regulatory-information functions — any material beyond those essential functions is prohibited or penalized. — EU 32025R0040, EE 113032019103, FI 2021/714, FI 2011/646
  • Eliminate single-use plastic packaging components lacking essential technical function (required). Audit every plastic packaging element against a defined necessity test (protection, sanitary/integrity, transport, regulatory information); components that fail the test must be removed, targeting full elimination by 31 December 2025. — FR JORFTEXT000043458675
  • Ensure packaging material, structure, and cost are proportionate to the product (required). Packaging specifications — material choice, structural complexity, and cost — must be demonstrably proportionate to the quality, specifications, and cost of the contained product; disproportionate (excessive) packaging is prohibited. — CN 4028abcc61277793016127e112bb1ff4, CN ff8081818d736e08018d786bd28914b5, CN ff80818180e0a4410180f4acf8e12c02
  • Right-size consumer-unit ready-to-sell packages to ≤50 kg per unit (required). Products sold directly to final consumers must be packaged in ready-to-sell units that do not exceed 50 kg in weight, establishing a hard upper-bound for consumer-facing pack sizes. — EU 32021R1929
  • Adopt lightweight or compact housings and components in electronics and appliances (required). For electronics, appliances, and similar durable goods, design teams must rationalize raw-material use by specifying smaller or lighter housings, compressors, drive units, and structural parts to suppress end-of-life waste generation. — JP 413M60000400062, JP 413M60000400063, JP 413M60000400066, JP 413M60000400072
  • Design paper carryout bags with basis weight ≤30 lb to qualify for fee exemption (exemption). In Colorado, paper bags at or below a 30-pound basis weight fall outside the statutory definition of 'carryout bag,' avoiding associated fee obligations; specify this threshold in bag procurement and design briefs. — CO HB-21-1162

Targets to hit

  • 50% reduction in single-use plastic beverage bottles placed on market by 2030 (FR cenv/L541-10-11)
  • 100% elimination of unnecessary single-use plastic packaging components by 31 December 2025 (FR JORFTEXT000043458675)
  • ≤50 kg per ready-to-sell consumer-unit package (EU 32021R1929)
  • Paper carryout bag basis weight ≤30 lb for fee exemption (CO HB-21-1162)
Evidence (statutory language)
"Bags with paper basis weight of 30 pounds or less are excluded from the definition of 'carryout bag'"
— CO HB-21-1162 (enacted, Exemption available)
"Such products must be packaged in ready-to-sell packages of not more than 50 kg in weight for use by the final consumer"
— EU 32021R1929 (enacted, Required)
"Producers must contribute to a 50% reduction by 2030 in the number of single-use plastic beverage bottles placed on the market"
— FR cenv/L541-10-11 (enacted, Required)

Shift to reusable / refillable formats

The 'reuse/refill' lever shifts packaging and product formats away from single-use toward designs that complete multiple life cycles through refilling, washing, or rotation. Acting now secures EPR fee reductions, exemptions from deposit and collection obligations, and compliance with enacted reuse targets across the US, EU, and key trading-partner markets.

Backed by 106 bills (103 enacted) — Required in 25 state(s) · Exemption available in 16 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 10 state(s) · Referenced in 5 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Design beverage containers for washing, refilling, and resale at scale (required). Glass beverage containers must be engineered to withstand processor washing and re-sale cycles; all beverage containers sold in California must be either refillable or recyclable, with refillable glass containers carrying a refund value. — CA AB-962, CA 406_97_pit
  • Engineer reusable packaging to survive at least two full trips or rotations (required). Any packaging designated as 'reusable' must be structurally designed for a minimum of two trips or rotations and must include a tracking mechanism to record each completed cycle, satisfying the baseline reuse definition under enacted packaging regulations. — IE eli/2007/si/798/made/en, IE eli/2014/si/282/made/en, EU 32025R0040
  • Design gas cylinders and pressure vessels for deposit-return and multi-refill lifecycles (required). Gas cylinders and propane/industrial bottles must be designed to enable multiple refills over their full lifecycle and must be paired with a deposit-return scheme or equivalent take-back system as a condition of market placement; the same principle applies to portable propane cylinders, which require stewardship plans covering refill and residual-gas capture. — FR cenv/L541-10-24, FR JORFTEXT000026863961, FR JORFTEXT000032770063, CT HB-5142
  • Qualify reusable carryout bags by meeting minimum durability and capacity specifications (exemption). Reusable carryout bags must be manufactured for at least 125 uses, carry a minimum of 22 lbs over 175 feet, feature stitched handles, and be made of cloth, fiber, fabric, or recycled material (e.g., PET) to be excluded from single-use plastic bag fee obligations. — CO HB-21-1162
  • Design textiles to enable preparation for reuse and meet escalating annual reuse targets (required). Textile products placed on the market must be designed (construction, fasteners, material durability) to facilitate preparation-for-reuse sorting and processing, enabling operators to meet the 20% reuse weight target in 2025 rising to 25% from 2030 onward. — NL BWBR0048093
  • Adopt reusable or refillable packaging formats to unlock EPR fee advantages and target exemptions (fee-advantaged). Packaging formally classified as reusable (meeting eco-organism standards where applicable) qualifies for lower EPR eco-modulated fees, is charged only once upon initial market entry in reuse-system PRO frameworks, and is excluded from collection/recovery tonnage targets — making reuse a direct cost lever alongside compliance. — FR cenv/L541-10-3, MN HF-3911, EU 32018L0852, CL 1157019, AT 20008902

Targets to hit

  • ≥125 uses minimum design life for reusable carryout bags (CO HB-21-1162)
  • ≥2 trips or rotations minimum for reusable packaging (IE eli/2007/si/798/made/en, IE eli/2014/si/282/made/en)
  • 40% annual reuse rate for pallet wrappings and straps (EU 32026D0429)
  • 20% of single-use plastic packaging tonnage reduced by 31 Dec 2025 vs. 2018 baseline, with ≥50% of that reduction via reuse (FR JORFTEXT000043458675)
  • 20% of textile products prepared for reuse by weight in 2025, rising to 25% from 2030 (NL BWBR0048093)
  • ≥50 micrometer film thickness for reusable plastic bags (JP 418M60000740001)
  • ≥10 litre capacity for refillable containers to qualify for deposit exemption (CA 406_97_pit)
Evidence (statutory language)
"Reusable carryout bags (designed/manufactured for at least 125 uses, carry at least 22 lbs over 175 feet, stitched handles, made of cloth/fiber/fabric or recycled material such as PET) are excluded from 'single-use plastic carryout bag' definition"
— CO HB-21-1162 (enacted, Exemption available)
"Supermarkets, self-service stores, warehouses, and general commercial establishments must progressively replace non-reusable polymeric bags with reusable bags or alternatives within 36 months of the law's entry into force."
— PE ley-30884 (enacted, Required)
"Economic operators using pallet wrappings and straps are subject to the overall 40% re-use target under Article 29(1) of Regulation (EU) 2025/40 for transport packaging formats in a calendar year (not exempted by this Decision)"
— EU 32026D0429 (enacted, Required)

Eliminate restricted substances (PFAS, heavy metals, etc.)

Toxics elimination requires design teams to remove or strictly limit PFAS, heavy metals (Pb, Cd, Hg, Cr VI), and brominated flame retardants from products, packaging, and components before market entry. Multiple enacted laws across packaging, electronics, batteries, vehicles, and textiles set hard concentration ceilings or outright bans, making early material substitution the lowest-risk path.

Backed by 248 bills (242 enacted) — Required in 31 state(s) · Exemption available in 16 state(s) · Prohibited in 15 state(s) · Referenced in 12 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 8 state(s) · Fee-penalized in 4 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Strip lead, mercury, cadmium, and Cr VI from all packaging materials (required). The combined concentration of Pb, Cd, Hg, and hexavalent chromium in any packaging or packaging component must not exceed 100 ppm (mg/kg) by weight; audit every substrate, ink, pigment, and coating in your packaging supply chain against this ceiling. — NL BWBR0018139, LV 57207, PL DU/2019/542, PL DU/2024/927, AT 20008902, DK lta/2025/1146, EE 113032019103
  • Remove lead, mercury, cadmium, PBB, PBDE, and Cr VI from all electronics (required). No homogeneous material in electrical or electronic equipment may contain these six restricted substances above the maximum concentration values in Annex II of the RoHS Directive; map every component to a homogeneous-material level and obtain supplier declarations of conformity. — EU 32011L0065, JP 413M60000400086, JP 413M60000400083
  • Eliminate mercury and cadmium from all battery chemistries you specify (prohibited). Mercury must not exceed 0.0005 wt% in any battery (all types except button cells, which carry a separate exemption up to 2 wt%); cadmium must not exceed 0.002 wt% in portable batteries, including those built into devices; select alternative chemistries at the cell-selection stage. — AT 20005815, NL BWBR0024492, EU 32006L0066, ES BOE-A-2008-2387
  • Design vehicles and auto components free of Pb, Hg, Cd, and Cr VI (prohibited). Vehicles and components placed on the market must contain no lead, mercury, cadmium, or hexavalent chromium; establish contractual substance-restriction requirements with every tier of your automotive supply chain and verify compliance through material data sheets. — DE altautov, EU 32009L0001
  • Specify PFAS-free fibers, backings, and treatments for all carpet products (prohibited). No carpet sold in New York may contain or be treated with any PFAS chemical; eliminate PFAS-based stain-resist, water-resist, and soil-resist treatments from carpet specifications and qualify alternative chemistries before the statutory deadline. — NY S-5027C
  • Never intentionally add mercury to any product or sub-assembly (prohibited). Mercury-added products are absolutely prohibited from manufacture and from incorporation into assembled products; conduct a formulation review at the design stage to confirm no mercury-containing components (switches, lamps, sensors) are specified. — AU F2021L01393

Targets to hit

  • Pb + Cd + Hg + Cr VI ≤ 100 ppm by weight in packaging (NL BWBR0018139, LV 57207, PL DU/2019/542, PL DU/2024/927, AT 20008902, DK lta/2025/1146, EE 113032019103)
  • Mercury ≤ 0.0005 wt% in all batteries except button cells (AT 20005815, NL BWBR0024492, EU 32006L0066, ES BOE-A-2008-2387)
  • Cadmium ≤ 0.002 wt% in portable batteries including built-in types (AT 20005815, NL BWBR0024492, EU 32006L0066, ES BOE-A-2008-2387)
  • Lead, Hg, Cr VI ≤ 0.1% by weight per homogeneous material in EEE (EU 32002D0525)
  • PCB + PCT ≤ 50 ppm in regenerated base oils at entry into force; 0 ppm one year after (ES BOE-A-2006-9832)
  • Button-cell mercury exemption: ≤ 2 wt% mercury permitted (ES BOE-A-2008-2387)
Evidence (statutory language)
"Absolute prohibition on manufacture of mercury-added products"
— AU F2021L01393 (enacted, Prohibited)
"Absolute prohibition on incorporation of mercury-added products into assembled products"
— AU F2021L01393 (enacted, Prohibited)
"do not place on market batteries containing more than 0.0005 wt% mercury (regardless of whether built into devices)"
— AT 20005815 (enacted, Required)

Avoid banned / restricted materials and formats

Material restriction requires design teams to eliminate banned materials and formats before products reach market, since multiple enacted laws across jurisdictions prohibit specific plastics, formats, and material types outright. Acting early avoids redesign costs, supply-chain disruption, and legal non-compliance across the US, EU, UK, and key trading-partner markets.

Backed by 132 bills (127 enacted) — Prohibited in 27 state(s) · Exemption available in 16 state(s) · Required in 13 state(s) · Fee-penalized in 10 state(s) · Referenced in 8 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Eliminate single-use plastic items: bags, straws, cutlery, plates, stirrers, balloon sticks (prohibited). Multiple enacted laws prohibit single-use plastic carryout bags (CO HB-21-1162, AT 20002086), polymeric-base straws, plates, cups, and utensils (PE ley-30884), plastic cutlery, stirrers, and balloon sticks (UK ssi/2021/410, DE ewkverbotsv); remove all such formats from product and packaging design. — CO HB-21-1162, PE ley-30884, UK ssi/2021/410, DE ewkverbotsv, AT 20002086
  • Replace expanded polystyrene (EPS) in all food and beverage containers (prohibited). EPS is prohibited for food containers, beverage containers, and cups under multiple enacted laws; substitute with paper-based, compostable fibre, or other non-EPS materials for any food-service or beverage packaging format. — DE ewkverbotsv, PE ley-30884, UK ssi/2021/410
  • Never use oxo-degradable plastic in any product or packaging (prohibited). Oxo-degradable plastics are outright prohibited under enacted law; do not specify them as a 'degradable' alternative to conventional plastic in any product category or market. — DE ewkverbotsv, AT 20002086
  • Specify PET, steel, or aluminium for single-use beverage bottle formats (required). Enacted deposit-return scheme legislation restricts eligible beverage containers to PET plastic, steel, or aluminium (150 ml–3 L); non-PET plastic bottles and glass are excluded from scheme eligibility, limiting their market viability. — UK ssi/2025/188, IE eli/2024/si/33/made/en
  • Phase out non-recyclable, non-energy-recoverable multi-layered plastic packaging (prohibited). Enacted Indian law requires the manufacture and use of multi-layered plastic with no recyclability or energy-recovery pathway to be phased out within two years; audit all multi-layer laminate structures and transition to mono-material or recoverable alternatives. — IN 183721
  • Ensure any biodegradable plastic claim conforms to certified compostability standards (required). Where biodegradable plastic is used as a substitute material, enacted Indian law requires conformance to IS 17899 T:2022; verify certification before specifying biodegradable resins to avoid greenwashing liability. — IN 227249

Targets to hit

  • Plastic carrier bags must be ≥50 µm thickness or replaced entirely with paper/cardboard/bioplastic by 2025-01-01 (LV 57207, PL DU/2017/2056)
  • Thin plastic carrier bags (<50 µm) must not be distributed free of charge; very thin bags (<15 µm) exempt only for hygiene/loose-food primary packaging (EE 113032019103)
  • Packaged products must contain no more than 90% by volume of meat-and-bone meal of Category 2 material (EU 32021R1929)
Evidence (statutory language)
"On and after January 1, 2024: Do NOT provide single-use plastic carryout bags to customers (with limited inventory exception through June 1, 2024)"
— CO HB-21-1162 (enacted, Prohibited)
"Single-use plastic cotton buds (Wattestäbchen) made wholly or partly of plastic"
— DE ewkverbotsv (enacted, Prohibited)
"Food containers made of expanded polystyrene (EPS) intended for immediate consumption"
— DE ewkverbotsv (enacted, Prohibited)

Apply required recyclability / disposal labeling

Labeling and marking requirements mandate that products and packaging carry specific recyclability, material-identity, and disposal symbols to enable correct sorting and end-of-life handling. Design teams must embed these marks at the component level—not just on outer packaging—to satisfy enacted law across batteries, plastics, electronics, and beverage containers.

Backed by 277 bills (262 enacted) — Required in 40 state(s) · Referenced in 11 state(s) · Exemption available in 9 state(s) · Fee-penalized in 4 state(s) · Prohibited in 2 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 2 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Print crossed-out wheeled bin symbol on all batteries; add Hg, Cd, or Pb chemical symbol beneath it when heavy-metal thresholds are exceeded (required). The chemical symbol must appear below the collection mark and occupy at least one quarter of that mark's area; all marks must be visible, legible, and indelible on the battery itself (not only on packaging). — EU 32006L0066, UK uksi/1994/232, NL BWBR0024492, FI 2014/520, PL DU/2015/687, UK nisr/1995/122
  • Size the separate-collection mark on batteries to cover at least 3% of the largest side (required). The mark may not exceed 5 cm × 5 cm; for cylindrical cells use 3% of half the surface area (max 5 cm × 5 cm); if the resulting mark would be smaller than 0.5 cm × 0.5 cm, place a 1 cm × 1 cm mark on the packaging instead. — UK uksi/1994/232
  • Mark material names on all synthetic resin parts weighing 100 grams or more (required). For plastic vehicle components use ISO 1043-1, ISO 1043-2, and ISO 11469 codes; for elastomer vehicle components over 200 g use ISO 1629 codes; the mark must appear on the part itself to facilitate sorting for recycling. — JP 413M60000400076, JP 413M60000400080, JP 413M60000400085, JP 413M60000400086, JP 413M60000400088, JP 413M60000400090, JP 413M60000400091, JP 413M60000C00001, EU 32003D0138
  • Label recycled or biodegradable plastic packaging with content percentage or degradation specifics (required). Recycled plastic packaging must state the percentage of recycled content and conform to IS 14534:2023; biodegradable plastic packaging must state the number of days to degrade and the intended recipient environment. — IN 227249
  • Mark deposit-eligible beverage containers with the required deposit word-mark at minimum 1.2 mm font; never label a returnable container as non-returnable (required). Plastic bottles must bear 'Statiegeldfles' and metal beverage packaging must bear 'Statiegeld' in at least 1.2 mm font; placing any label, mark, stamp, or inscription indicating a container cannot be returned for a refund is prohibited. — NL BWBR0037392, CA 406_97_pit
  • Mark products and packaging with reuse or disposal method information; indicate post-August-2005 market placement on EEE (required). Producers must clearly mark reuse or disposal instructions on the product or its packaging; electrical and electronic equipment placed on the market after 13 August 2005 must carry a marking indicating that date to support WEEE sorting. — CN 4028abcc61277793016128016d2f42e7, EU 32012L0019

Targets to hit

  • Heavy-metal chemical symbol must occupy ≥25% of the collection mark's area on batteries (UK uksi/1994/232, NL BWBR0024492)
  • Separate-collection mark must cover ≥3% of battery's largest side, max 5 cm × 5 cm (UK uksi/1994/232)
  • Hg threshold triggering symbol: >0.0005 wt% (NL BWBR0024492)
  • Cd threshold triggering symbol: >0.002 wt% (NL BWBR0024492)
  • Pb threshold triggering symbol: >0.004 wt% (NL BWBR0024492)
  • Synthetic resin part material-name marking threshold: ≥100 g (JP 413M60000400076 et al.)
  • Elastomer vehicle component ISO-code marking threshold: >200 g (EU 32003D0138)
  • Plastic vehicle component ISO-code marking threshold: >100 g (EU 32003D0138)

Use certified-compostable materials where specified

Certified-compostable materials can substitute for recyclability across packaging and select product categories, unlocking regulatory exemptions and satisfying mandatory recyclability-or-compostability requirements. Design teams must act now because enacted laws in California, Indiana, and the EU already impose certification standards, labeling rules, and end-of-life performance obligations that take effect as early as 2032.

Backed by 29 bills (29 enacted) — Required in 9 state(s) · Exemption available in 4 state(s) · Referenced in 2 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 2 state(s) · Prohibited in 1 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Certify compostable packaging to EN 13432:2000 or IS/ISO 17088:2021 (required). Compostable plastic packaging sold in Indiana must conform to IS/ISO 17088:2021; EU packaging must comply with EN 13432:2000 to be presumed compliant with essential packaging requirements — select whichever standard applies to your target market, or both for dual-market products. — IN 227249, EU 32001D0524
  • Label industrial-compostable plastics accurately; never claim home-compostable (required). Indiana law requires compostable plastic packaging to bear the label 'compostable only under industrial composting'; French law simultaneously prohibits using the unqualified label 'compostable' on plastics that can only break down in industrial units — align labeling to both by using the qualified phrase. — IN 227249, FR JORFTEXT000041553759
  • Ensure all covered packaging is recyclable or compostable by 2032 in California (required). CA SB-54 requires that covered material offered for sale, distributed, or imported into California on or after January 1, 2032, is either recyclable or compostable — audit your portfolio now and roadmap compostability certification for any SKU that cannot meet recyclability. — CA SB-54
  • Design certified-compostable packaging to escape California collection and management obligations (exemption). California regulation 210391 exempts certified-compostable products and packaging from collection obligations, alternative collection system registration, management requirement calculations, and promotion and education obligations — obtain certification before compliance deadlines to qualify. — CA regulation/210391
  • Make fruit and vegetable labels home-compostable and bio-based (required). French law prohibits labels applied directly to fruits and vegetables unless they are compostable in home composting conditions and made wholly or partly of bio-based materials — this sector-specific rule requires a higher compostability bar than industrial certification alone. — FR JORFTEXT000042753962
  • Design carpet as compostable per CA Health & Safety Code Section 42357 to exit EPR coverage (exemption). CA AB-863 exempts carpet from covered-product obligations if it meets the compostability requirements of Section 42357 and is labeled accordingly — engage your carpet product team to evaluate this pathway as an alternative to EPR compliance. — CA AB-863

  • Agricultural film must be 100% biodegradable to avoid mandatory collection and delivery obligations (CN ff8081818a1cb709018a24ff766d20df)

Part 2 — Product design & right to repair

Adjacent legislation: right-to-repair and durability laws don't use EPR fees, but they impose design obligations on the same product teams — and they are the fastest-moving category in this dataset.

Design for repairability, spare-parts access, and longevity

Design for repairability, spare-parts access, and longevity requires that products be engineered so owners and independent repairers can diagnose, fix, and extend product life using available parts, documentation, and software. Multiple enacted US state laws (California, Colorado, Oregon) and international regulations now impose legally binding obligations on manufacturers across electronics, appliances, furniture, and mobility equipment—making this lever a compliance necessity, not a voluntary aspiration.

Backed by 147 bills (142 enacted) — Required in 24 state(s) · Exemption available in 14 state(s) · Referenced in 11 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 9 state(s) · Prohibited in 3 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Make parts, documentation, firmware, and tools available to all repairers (required). Provide owners and independent repair providers with all documentation, functional parts, embedded software, firmware, and diagnostic tools on fair and reasonable terms—and supply software tools at no charge when needed to enable full functionality. — CA SB-244, CA SB-1384, CO HB-24-1121, CO HB-23-1011, CO HB-22-1031
  • Eliminate parts pairing that blocks, degrades, or misleads repair (prohibited). Do not use software or hardware pairing to prevent installation of functional replacement parts, reduce device performance after non-OEM parts are installed, or display non-dismissible misleading alerts about unidentified parts—all prohibited for consumer equipment first sold in Oregon after January 1, 2025. — OR SB-550
  • Standardize components across models and specify high-durability parts (required). Design circuit boards, structural components (columns, shelves, tabletops, legs), drive units, and compressors for maximum service life and specify them as common across product lines so a single replacement part serves multiple models, reducing obsolescence-driven disposal. — JP 413M60000400063, JP 413M60000400070, JP 413M60000400072, JP 413M60000400073
  • Stage spare-parts availability from launch through end-of-production window (required). For electronics (e.g., laptops and mobile phones), make critical parts such as displays, batteries, and chargers available from first market launch; provide secondary parts (motherboards, connectors, fans) within 2 years; and maintain all listed parts for at least 5 years after the last unit of a model is sold. — CA SB-244, FR JORFTEXT000044806559, FR JORFTEXT000045072860, FI 2021/714
  • Ensure timely repair capability for mobility and assistive equipment (required). For wheelchairs and mobility aids, design service logistics so that any unit sold or leased can be repaired within 10 business days of a repair request, and maintain spare-parts availability for at least 5 years post-market launch of the last model unit. — CT SB-308, FR JORFTEXT000045072860
  • Back product longevity claims with documented warranties and repairability scoring (required). For carpet, provide an all-inclusive 7-year warranty with every sale; for electrical and electronic equipment, calculate and publish a repairability index score (out of 10) using defined weighted sub-criteria, and provide repair and maintenance information to service centres within one year of first market placement. — NY S-5027C, FR JORFTEXT000042837821, LU eli/etat/leg/loi/2022/06/09/a266/jo/fr

Methodology & limits
  • Source. 605 bills across 46 states classified and analyzed by Atlas Circular; design signals extracted from each bill's compliance text and verified to be verbatim before inclusion (no paraphrased citations).
  • Authority. Enacted laws are current obligations; proposed bills indicate direction and may change or fail. Each principle notes how many of its backing bills are enacted.
  • Coverage. Drawn from the bills with full compliance extractions to date — strong on flagship enacted programs (CA, OR, CO, ME, CT, MN, VT), lighter on early-stage bills.
  • Costs. No fee or cost figures are asserted; eco-modulation rates are set by producer responsibility organizations (e.g. Circular Action Alliance) and are pending. Once published, each design lever maps to fee exposure based on the volume of covered product you sell.
  • This is design guidance, not legal advice. Confirm obligations against the statute and your counsel for the states you operate in.
