Designing for Circularity: A Practitioner's Guide

SignalScout · Circularity Intelligence · Compiled 13 June 2026

How to design packaging and products that stay compliant — and pay lower fees — under US Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) and adjacent circular-economy law.

Derived from 80 bills across 13 states (44 already enacted) that our system has classified and analyzed. Every principle below traces to specific bills, with the exact statutory language quoted. Proposed bills show the direction of travel; enacted laws are obligations today.

How to use this guide. Each lever is a design decision you control. For every lever: the *imperatives* are what to do, *targets* are the hard numbers to hit, and *evidence* shows the laws driving it. Audit your SKUs lever by lever; prioritize the states you sell into. Fee figures are intentionally omitted until the Circular Action Alliance publishes eco-modulation rates — at which point design choices map directly to dollar exposure.

Part 1 — Packaging design

Design packaging to be recyclable in available systems

Design for recycling requires that packaging and products be compatible with available collection, sorting, and end-market systems before they reach consumers. Multiple enacted state laws now mandate recyclability as a market-entry condition or tie fee levels to recyclability performance, making this lever both a compliance requirement and a cost-management tool.

Backed by 37 bills (17 enacted) — Required in 9 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 6 state(s) · Exemption available in 2 state(s) · Referenced in 2 state(s) · Fee-penalized in 1 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Ensure packaging is recyclable in programs covering ≥60% of CA population before labeling (required). Before displaying a chasing-arrows symbol, confirm the material type and form is collected by California recycling programs covering at least 60% of the state's population and routinely becomes feedstock for new products or packaging. — CA SB-343, CA SB-54
  • Design packaging to meet state recyclability standards in CO, ME, and OR systems (required). Covered packaging sold in Colorado, Maine, and Oregon must use readily recyclable materials compatible with those states' available collection and processing infrastructure; Maine's EPR program also offers an exemption for packaging demonstrating a ≥65% recycling rate for three consecutive years prior to January 1, 2027. — CO HB-22-1355, ME LD-1423, OR SB-582
  • Improve packaging recyclability to reduce eco-modulated EPR fee penalties (fee-advantaged). In Maine and proposed Oregon and Vermont packaging EPR programs, fees are adjusted based on recyclability (among other factors), so increasing a package's recyclability can lower the producer's fee obligation. — ME LD-1541, OR HB-3780, VT H-142
  • Design batteries and electronics to enable end-of-life collection and recycling (required). Batteries must be designed for easy user removal with no more than common household tools (CA AB-2440, VT S-254), and electronic smoking devices must be designed to support collection, recycling, and material recovery (ME LD-1519); batteries collected under stewardship programs must be managed by certified recyclers (MD SB-686). — CA AB-2440, VT S-254, ME LD-1519, MD SB-686
  • Design apparel and textiles to enable sorting and recycling at end of life (required). Under California's covered-apparel stewardship framework, products must support downstream collection, sorting, and recycling processes, meaning material choices and construction should not impede mechanical or chemical recycling pathways. — CA SB-707
  • Design beverage containers, tires, and gas cylinders for certified recycling pathways (required). Beverage containers must be recyclable in certified deposit-return systems (CA AB-1311, RI SB-996, RI HB-6207, VT H-915); tires must have a verified end-of-life recycling or resale pathway (CT HB-5352, CT HB-6486); and gas cylinders must support refilling or recycling with residual gas capture (CT HB-5142, NY A-8195). — CA AB-1311, RI SB-996, RI HB-6207, VT H-915, CT HB-5352, CT HB-6486, CT HB-5142, NY A-8195

Targets to hit

  • ≥60% of CA population served by recycling programs for chasing-arrows label eligibility (CA SB-343)
  • 100% of covered material recyclable or compostable by January 1, 2032 (CA SB-54)
  • ≥65% recycling rate for 3 consecutive years prior to January 1, 2027 for ME EPR exemption (ME LD-1423)
  • ≥70% recycling rate annually on and after January 1, 2027 for continued ME EPR exemption (ME LD-1423)
Evidence (statutory language)
"Ensure that any product or packaging displaying a chasing arrows symbol meets statewide recyclability criteria (collected for recycling by programs covering at least 60% of California's population) and is of a material type and form that routinely becomes feedstock used in production of new products or packaging"
— CA SB-343 (enacted, Required)
"Ensure covered material offered for sale, distributed, or imported in or into the state on or after January 1, 2032, is recyclable or compostable"
— CA SB-54 (enacted, Required)
"Batteries designed to be easily removed from a product by the user with no more than common household tools"
— CA AB-2440 (enacted, Required)

Incorporate post-consumer recycled content

Incorporating post-consumer recycled (PCR) content is increasingly mandated by enacted US state laws across plastic beverage containers, glass containers, paper bags, and plastic packaging, with specific minimum thresholds already in force or scheduled. Beyond compliance, multiple enacted and proposed extended-producer-responsibility (EPR) frameworks eco-modulate fees downward for higher recycled content, creating a direct financial incentive to exceed minimums.

Backed by 13 bills (7 enacted) — Fee-advantaged in 5 state(s) · Required in 3 state(s) · Exemption available in 2 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Hit staged PCR minimums in plastic beverage containers now and plan ahead (required). Plastic beverage containers sold in Maine must average at least 25% PCR plastic from 2026–2030, rising to 30% from 2031; California's SB-1013 imposes its own PCR schedule for filled plastic beverage containers, with a two-year compliance extension for box, bladder, and pouch wine/spirits containers. — ME LD-1467, CA SB-1013
  • Achieve 65% recycled content in plastic covered material by 2032 (required). California's SB-54 requires that plastic covered material meet a recycled content rate of not less than 65% on and after January 1, 2032; design teams should audit current resin sourcing and establish supplier contracts well in advance. — CA SB-54
  • Use minimum 35% post-filled glass in glass food and beverage containers (required). California's SB-38 mandates that glass container manufacturers incorporate at least 35% post-filled (recycled) glass in all glass food, drink, or beverage containers; specify this requirement in glass supplier agreements. — CA SB-38
  • Meet SABRC minimum PCR percentages to qualify for California state procurement (fee-advantaged). California's AB-661 requires products in revised SABRC categories to meet minimum postconsumer recycled content percentages; state agencies may purchase recycled products at up to 10% greater total cost than non-recycled alternatives, making compliance a competitive advantage for public-sector sales. — CA AB-661
  • Maximize recycled content in all packaging to reduce eco-modulated EPR fees (fee-advantaged). Enacted EPR laws in Maine (LD-1541) and proposed frameworks in Vermont (H-915, H-142) and Oregon (HB-3780) all reference recycled content as a factor that lowers producer fee obligations; increasing PCR content across packaging formats is a direct lever to reduce ongoing fee liability. — ME LD-1541, VT H-915, VT H-142, OR HB-3780
  • Specify 100% recycled content for paper carryout bags to ensure compliance (exemption). In Colorado, paper carryout bags qualify as a compliant alternative to single-use plastic only if made from 100% recycled material or post-consumer content with a basis weight greater than 30 pounds; design briefs for retail bags must reflect this threshold. — CO HB-21-1162

Targets to hit

  • 25% PCR plastic in plastic beverage containers 2026–2030 (ME LD-1467)
  • 30% PCR plastic in plastic beverage containers from 2031 (ME LD-1467)
  • 65% recycled content in plastic covered material from January 1, 2032 (CA SB-54)
  • 35% post-filled glass in glass food/drink/beverage containers (CA SB-38)
  • 100% recycled material or post-consumer content in paper carryout bags (CO HB-21-1162)
  • State procurement preference when recycled product cost is within 10% of non-recycled alternative (CA AB-661)
Evidence (statutory language)
"Beginning January 1, 2026 through December 31, 2030: ensure that the total number of plastic beverage containers sold, offered for sale, or distributed for sale in Maine contain, on average and in the aggregate, at least 25% post-consumer recycled plastic"
— ME LD-1467 (enacted, Required)
"Beginning January 1, 2031: ensure that the total number of plastic beverage containers sold, offered for sale, or distributed for sale in Maine contain, on average and in the aggregate, at least 30% post-consumer recycled plastic"
— ME LD-1467 (enacted, Required)
"Recycled paper carryout bags (bags made from 100% recycled material or post-consumer content, with basis weight greater than 30 pounds)"
— CO HB-21-1162 (enacted, Exemption available)

Reduce packaging material per unit (lightweight, right-size)

Source reduction means designing packaging to use less material per unit through lightweighting, right-sizing, and eliminating unnecessary layers. Multiple enacted US laws directly require or financially reward material reduction, making it both a compliance obligation and a cost-management lever.

Backed by 10 bills (7 enacted) — Exemption available in 3 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 2 state(s) · Required in 2 state(s) · Referenced in 2 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Reduce single-use plastic packaging material amount per unit sold (required). CA SB-54 explicitly requires producers to source reduce plastic covered material, making this a direct legal obligation for plastic packaging sold in California. — CA SB-54
  • Minimize total packaging material to prevent negative environmental impacts (required). CO HB-22-1355 requires covered materials to be designed and managed to prevent or minimize negative environmental, social, economic, and health impacts, establishing minimization as a baseline design standard in Colorado. — CO HB-22-1355
  • Reduce packaging weight per unit to lower weight-based producer fee assessments (fee-advantaged). Maine's EPR program assesses producer fees in per-ton increments based on packaging weight sold into the state, so every gram removed from a pack format directly reduces fee liability. — ME LD-1541
  • Integrate source reduction into waste diversion planning for packaging lines (required). CA AB-1857 requires solid waste to be subject to source reduction and recycling elements of integrated waste management plans, meaning packaging decisions must be documented within broader waste diversion strategies. — CA AB-1857
  • Reduce paper bag basis weight to 30 lbs or below to avoid carryout bag fees (exemption). CO HB-21-1162 excludes paper bags with a basis weight of 30 pounds or less from the statutory definition of 'carryout bag,' allowing qualifying lightweight paper bags to escape associated fee obligations. — CO HB-21-1162
  • Scope industrial packaging to exclusive manufacturing use to qualify for exemption (exemption). OR SB-582 exempts specialty packaging items used exclusively in industrial or manufacturing processes—such as cores and wraps for mill rolls or transport trays for component parts—so redesigning or reclassifying such packaging to meet this exclusive-use criterion avoids coverage. — OR SB-582

Targets to hit

  • Paper bag basis weight ≤ 30 lbs to escape carryout bag definition and fees (CO HB-21-1162)
  • Area rugs ≤ 11 ft × 15 ft to avoid product coverage (CA AB-863)
Evidence (statutory language)
"Bags with paper basis weight of 30 pounds or less are excluded from the definition of 'carryout bag'"
— CO HB-21-1162 (enacted, Exemption available)
"Payments assessed and collected from producers based on the weight (tons) of packaging material sold, offered for sale or distributed for sale in or into Maine by each producer."
— ME LD-1541 (enacted, Fee-advantaged)
"Excludes area rugs 11 feet by 15 feet or smaller"
— CA AB-863 (enacted, Exemption available)

Shift to reusable / refillable formats

The 'reuse/refill' lever shifts product and packaging design away from single-use formats toward durable, washable, or refillable systems that can complete multiple use cycles. Multiple enacted US laws now mandate or incentivize reuse-ready design across beverage containers, pressurized gas cylinders, and paint products, making this lever both a compliance requirement and a fee-avoidance opportunity.

Backed by 8 bills (6 enacted) — Required in 3 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 1 state(s) · Referenced in 1 state(s) · Exemption available in 1 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Design glass beverage containers for certified washing and refill cycles (required). Beverage manufacturers in California must ensure their reusable glass containers are processed by a certified processor for washing and subsequent refill and sale, so containers must be engineered to withstand repeated commercial wash cycles without structural or label failure. — CA AB-962
  • Design pressurized gas cylinders for refill, refurbishment, and stewardship programs (required). Enacted law in Connecticut and Oregon requires cylinders to support refilling (including capture of residual gases) and participate in stewardship plans covering collection and end-of-life management; design for refill also unlocks statutory exemptions for LPG containers in Oregon. — CT HB-5142, OR SB-582, NY A-8195
  • Permanently mark brand name on refillable glass carbonated beverage containers (exemption). Maine law exempts refillable glass carbonated beverage containers bearing a permanently marked brand name and a refund value of at least 5¢ from standard label marking requirements, so embossing or molding the brand directly into the glass is the qualifying design move. — ME LD-1909
  • Build reusable carryout bags to minimum durability and capacity specifications (fee-advantaged). Colorado's enacted law excludes bags from the single-use plastic bag definition—and associated fees—only when they are designed and manufactured for at least 125 uses, can carry at least 22 lbs over 175 feet, have stitched handles, and are made of cloth, fiber, fabric, or recycled material such as PET. — CO HB-21-1162
  • Integrate reuse and recovery pathways into postconsumer paint product programs (required). California's enacted paint stewardship law requires the stewardship organization's plan to actively promote reuse of postconsumer paint products as a primary strategy before end-of-life management, so paint packaging and product formulation should support reuse-grade quality retention. — CA SB-1143

Targets to hit

  • ≥125 use cycles for reusable carryout bags to qualify for fee exemption (CO HB-21-1162)
  • ≥22 lb carry capacity over 175 feet for reusable carryout bags (CO HB-21-1162)
  • ≥5¢ refund value on refillable glass carbonated beverage containers for label exemption (ME LD-1909)
Evidence (statutory language)
"Reusable carryout bags (designed/manufactured for at least 125 uses, carry at least 22 lbs over 175 feet, stitched handles, made of cloth/fiber/fabric or recycled material such as PET) are excluded from 'single-use plastic carryout bag' definition"
— CO HB-21-1162 (enacted, Fee-advantaged)
"Beverage manufacturers must have their reusable glass beverage containers processed by a certified processor for subsequent washing for refill and sale"
— CA AB-962 (enacted, Required)
"Stewardship plan must provide for refilling or recycling cylinders and capturing residual gases for reuse"
— CT HB-5142 (enacted, Required)

Eliminate restricted substances (PFAS, heavy metals, etc.)

Toxics elimination requires removing restricted substances—mercury, PFAS, free-liquid electrolytes, and other hazardous materials—from product and packaging designs before they reach market. Enacted laws already prohibit or penalize these substances across thermostats, batteries, packaging, and turf; acting now avoids legal exposure and eco-modulated fee penalties in EPR programs.

Backed by 17 bills (7 enacted) — Prohibited in 4 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 4 state(s) · Referenced in 4 state(s) · Required in 3 state(s) · Exemption available in 3 state(s) · Fee-penalized in 1 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Eliminate mercury from all thermostat and switch designs immediately (required). Mercury-added thermostats and mercury switches are directly regulated under California law; design teams must transition to mercury-free alternatives for any thermostat sold in or into California. — CA AB-732, CA AB-707
  • Design batteries with no free-liquid electrolyte in any formulation (prohibited). Multiple enacted and advancing state laws (KY, VT, and others) prohibit or exclude from EPR programs batteries containing electrolyte as a free liquid; sealed, no-free-liquid designs are the only compliant path across these jurisdictions. — KY SB-49, VT S-254, CO SB-25-163, OR HB-4144
  • Remove hazardous substances from packaging to minimize eco-modulated EPR fees (fee-advantaged). Enacted and advancing packaging EPR programs in Maine and Oregon explicitly eco-modulate producer fees based on toxicity and hazardous substance content; eliminating these substances directly reduces fee burden. — ME LD-1541, OR HB-3780, VT H-142
  • Eliminate mercury from battery chemistry above allowable thresholds (prohibited). Proposed legislation in Minnesota would prohibit manufacture and sale of batteries containing mercury above allowable thresholds; design teams should audit battery chemistries now and substitute mercury-free alternatives to stay ahead of enactment. — MN HF-4565, MN SF-4679
  • Remove PFAS from artificial turf components and infill materials (required). New York legislation passed the chamber requiring compliance with PFAS restrictions applicable to artificial turf under amended ECL 27-3313; turf product designers must audit and eliminate PFAS-containing fibers, coatings, and infill. — NY S-10168
  • Reduce battery hazardous substance content to lower stewardship program fees (fee-advantaged). Advancing battery EPR bills in Minnesota and Oregon indicate fee structures will be eco-modulated based on battery chemistry, hazardous content, and recyclability; selecting lower-hazard chemistries now positions products for lower ongoing stewardship costs. — MN HF-4565, MN SF-4679, MD SB-686

Targets to hit

  • Sealed lead-acid rechargeable batteries: no liquid electrolyte and ≤11 pounds to qualify for EPR exemption (VT S-254)
Evidence (statutory language)
"mercury-added thermostats"
— CA AB-732 (enacted, Required)
"Batteries containing electrolyte as a free liquid"
— CT HB-5019 (enacted, Exemption available)
"Batteries containing an electrolyte as a free liquid, or products containing such batteries"
— KY SB-49 (enacted, Prohibited)

Avoid banned / restricted materials and formats

Material restriction requires design teams to eliminate banned materials and formats before products reach market, avoiding legal liability and producer-responsibility fees. Enacted state laws already prohibit specific materials—EPS foam, single-use plastic bags—and proposed legislation signals further restrictions across packaging, furniture, and electronics sectors.

Backed by 13 bills (8 enacted) — Prohibited in 5 state(s) · Referenced in 3 state(s) · Exemption available in 2 state(s) · Required in 1 state(s) · Fee-penalized in 1 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Eliminate expanded polystyrene (EPS) from all product and packaging formats (prohibited). Colorado law prohibits manufacture or distribution of EPS products—including blown polystyrene (Styrofoam) and extruded foam from thermoplastic petrochemical materials using styrene monomer—so substitute with fiber, molded pulp, or other non-EPS cushioning. — CO HB-21-1162
  • Remove single-use plastic carryout bags from all retail packaging systems (prohibited). Colorado enacted a ban effective January 1, 2024, and Vermont legislation in committee targets the same format; design teams should eliminate single-use plastic bags from packaging and retail fulfillment workflows across all markets. — CO HB-21-1162, VT H-142
  • Specify recyclable materials to minimize eco-modulated producer-responsibility fees (fee-advantaged). Vermont's extended producer responsibility framework allows fee structures to be modulated based on material type and recyclability, meaning non-recyclable material choices will attract higher fees; prioritize verified recyclable substrates at the design stage. — VT H-915
  • Design durable packaging for products with a ≥5-year use life to claim EPR exemption (exemption). Both Colorado and Maine enacted exemptions from producer-responsibility obligations for packaging intended to store or protect a durable product usable for at least five years; document intended product lifespan to substantiate the exemption claim. — CO HB-22-1355, ME LD-1541
  • Prohibit fiberglass-containing replacement components in children's products, mattresses, and upholstered furniture (prohibited). Proposed New York legislation would bar custom upholsterers from using any replacement component containing fiberglass when repairing or reupholstering children's products, mattresses, or upholstered furniture; design teams specifying repair-compatible materials should vet all fill and backing materials for fiberglass content. — NY A-7912
  • Avoid refillable glass label exemption for spirits, wine, and malt liquor containers (prohibited). Maine law explicitly excludes glass beverage containers holding spirits, wine, or malt liquor from the refillable glass container label exemption, so beverage designers in those categories must not rely on that pathway and should evaluate alternative compliance routes. — ME LD-1909

Targets to hit

  • Single-use plastic carryout bags banned by January 1, 2024 (CO HB-21-1162)
  • Durable packaging exemption threshold: ≥5 years intended product use life (CO HB-22-1355, ME LD-1541)
Evidence (statutory language)
"On and after January 1, 2024: Do NOT provide single-use plastic carryout bags to customers (with limited inventory exception through June 1, 2024)"
— CO HB-21-1162 (enacted, Prohibited)
"Materials from a large-scale biomass management event must not include whole or partial animal carcasses or animal byproducts other than manure"
— CA SB-279 (enacted, Prohibited)
"Expanded polystyrene (EPS) products including blown polystyrene (Styrofoam), expanded or extruded foam made from thermoplastic petrochemical materials utilizing styrene monomer"
— CO HB-21-1162 (enacted, Prohibited)

Apply required recyclability / disposal labeling

Labeling and marking requirements now span batteries, beverage containers, mattresses, home furnishings, and specialty products, with enacted laws in multiple states mandating specific on-product disclosures before sale. Design teams must audit every product category against jurisdiction-specific marking rules to avoid deceptive-claim prohibitions and ensure end-of-life handling is communicated directly on the product or its packaging.

Backed by 24 bills (11 enacted) — Required in 10 state(s) · Exemption available in 2 state(s) · Referenced in 2 state(s) · Prohibited in 1 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Mark all covered batteries with chemistry, producer ID, and disposal warning (required). Batteries (including those embedded in products) must bear the manufacturer or producer name/brand, battery chemistry type, and an explicit indication that the battery should not be disposed of as household waste — required across enacted laws in California, Kentucky, and Connecticut. — CA SB-1215, KY SB-49, CT HB-5019
  • Never place resin code inside chasing arrows unless product is genuinely recyclable (prohibited). Rigid plastic bottles and containers sold in California may not display a resin identification code inside a chasing arrows symbol unless the product meets statewide recyclability requirements; any deceptive or misleading recyclability claim — including unqualified chasing arrows — is prohibited. — CA SB-343
  • Emboss or permanently mark refund value and state identifier on every deposit beverage container (required). Refundable beverage containers sold in Maine must clearly show the refund value (or 'RV') and 'Maine'/'ME' via embossing, stamping, labeling, or secure attachment (not on the bottom); Oregon requires a refund value statement on the label, and California requires labeling per the Beverage Container Recycling and Litter Reduction Act — ensure brand identification remains legible on the container exterior in all deposit states. — ME LD-1909, OR SB-1520, CA SB-1013, CA AB-1311
  • Include flame retardant chemical statement on home furnishing and insulation product labels (required). Manufacturers of home furnishings and thermal insulation sold in California must include a flame retardant chemical statement directly on the product label, making this a mandatory on-product disclosure distinct from packaging recyclability labeling. — CA SB-814
  • Add consumer recycling education and free drop-off information to mattress labeling (required). California requires mattress manufacturers or stewardship program operators to educate consumers or ultimate end users about used mattress recycling and available free drop-off or collection options, which should be incorporated into hang tags, labels, or accompanying documentation. — CA AB-1478
  • Affix a prominent permanent fiberglass warning label on children's products, mattresses, and upholstered furniture containing fiberglass (required). Under proposed New York legislation, any new children's product, mattress, or upholstered furniture containing fiberglass must carry a prominent, permanent label warning that the product contains fiberglass which can be harmful if ingested, inhaled, or exposed to skin — design teams should anticipate this requirement in label real-estate planning now. — NY A-7912

Targets to hit

  • Refund value ≥ 5¢ permanently marked on refillable glass carbonated containers to qualify for label-marking exemption (ME LD-1909)
  • Newly covered wine/spirits boxes, bladders, and pouches exempt from California beverage container labeling until July 1, 2025 (CA SB-1013)

Use certified-compostable materials where specified

Compostability is a legally recognized alternative pathway to recyclability for covered materials in California, with deadlines and sector-specific exemptions already enacted. Design teams must act now to ensure materials meet certified-compostable standards before 2032 mandates take effect and to unlock available exemptions in carpets and organic-waste packaging.

Backed by 4 bills (4 enacted) — Exemption available in 1 state(s) · Required in 1 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Design covered packaging to be recyclable or certified-compostable by 2032 (required). All covered material offered for sale, distributed, or imported into California on or after January 1, 2032, must be either recyclable or compostable — compostability is a fully valid compliance path under CA SB-54. — CA SB-54
  • Design organic-waste packaging to be compostable to support disposal reduction (required). Packaging associated with organic waste must be designed for composting to contribute to California's 75% reduction in statewide organic-waste disposal from 2014 levels. — CA AB-1857
  • Label carpet 'compostable' per Section 42357 to secure product exemption (exemption). Carpet products that meet the compostability requirements of CA Public Resources Code Section 42357 and carry the corresponding label are excluded from covered-product obligations under CA AB-863. — CA AB-863
  • Restrict compostable feedstocks to green, agricultural, food, or vegetative materials (exemption). On-site composting operations using only green material, agricultural material, food material, or vegetative food material with total feedstock and compost not exceeding 200 cubic yards at any one time qualify as an excluded activity requiring no permit under CA SB-279. — CA SB-279

Targets to hit

  • 100% of covered material recyclable or compostable by January 1, 2032 (CA SB-54)
  • 75% reduction in statewide organic-waste disposal from 2014 levels (CA AB-1857)
  • ≤200 cubic yards total feedstock and compost onsite for permit-free composting exemption (CA SB-279)

Part 2 — Product design & right to repair

Adjacent legislation: right-to-repair and durability laws don't use EPR fees, but they impose design obligations on the same product teams — and they are the fastest-moving category in this dataset.

Design for repairability, spare-parts access, and longevity

Design for repairability requires that manufacturers make documentation, parts, software, and tools available to independent repairers and owners on fair, non-discriminatory terms for defined post-manufacture periods. Multiple enacted US state laws now impose legally binding obligations across consumer electronics, agricultural/industrial equipment, motor vehicles, and powered wheelchairs, making repair-access planning a core product-development requirement.

Backed by 25 bills (15 enacted) — Required in 8 state(s) · Exemption available in 7 state(s) · Referenced in 2 state(s) · Prohibited in 2 state(s) · Fee-advantaged in 1 state(s).

Design imperatives

  • Publish full repair documentation, parts, and tools post-manufacture (required). For consumer products with a wholesale price of $50–$99.99, make documentation, functional parts, and tools available for at least 3 years after the last manufacture date; for products at $100 or more, extend that window to at least 7 years — all on fair and reasonable terms. — CA SB-244
  • Provide software tools at no charge, without authorization or internet requirements (required). Software diagnostic and repair tools must be delivered at no charge, must not require manufacturer authorization or internet connectivity to function, must impose no access impediments, and must enable full device functionality — applicable across consumer electronics, agricultural equipment, and powered wheelchairs. — RI HB-5017, RI SB-884, CO HB-22-1031, CA SB-1384, NY S-4104
  • Price parts and tools equivalently for independent and authorized repairers (required). Parts, embedded software, firmware, and non-software tools must be offered to independent repair providers and owners at costs no greater than the manufacturer's suggested retail price and on terms no less favorable than those offered to authorized repair providers, accounting for all discounts and rebates. — NY S-4104, CO HB-22-1031, CA SB-1384, RI SB-884
  • Design motor vehicles with nonproprietary, standardized diagnostic interfaces (required). Vehicles must provide on-board diagnostic access through an off-the-shelf PC and a nonproprietary interface device complying with SAE J2534, SAE J1939, or ISO 22900 (or successors), and must incorporate an interoperable, owner-authorization access platform standardized across all makes and models. — ME LD-2211
  • Ensure powered wheelchair spare parts and security-reset tools are independently accessible (required). For Medi-Cal covered powered wheelchairs, manufacturers must make available at minimum batteries, chargers, joysticks, wheel assemblies, antitip devices, armrests, and caster spheres, plus any documentation or tools needed to reset electronic security locks during servicing; programmable or clinically calibrated positioning components are exempt. — CA SB-1384, CT SB-308
  • Never gate parts, tools, or documentation on an authorized-repair arrangement (required). Manufacturers must not condition the availability of any part, tool, or documentation on the recipient entering into an authorized repair provider agreement with the OEM, ensuring open access for independent repairers and end owners alike. — NY S-4104

Targets to hit

  • 3-year post-manufacture repair support for products with wholesale price $50–$99.99 (CA SB-244)
  • 7-year post-manufacture repair support for products with wholesale price $100 or more (CA SB-244)
  • Wheelchair repair completed within 10 business days of repair request (CT SB-308)
  • Wheelchair parts ordered within 3 business days of repair assessment (CT SB-308)
  • Interoperable owner-authorization vehicle access platform required by 2027 (ME LD-2211)
Evidence (statutory language)
"Manufacturers of products with wholesale price $50–$99.99 must make available to product owners, service and repair facilities, and service dealers sufficient documentation, functional parts, and tools (including updates) on fair and reasonable terms to effect diagnosis, maintenance, or repair for at least 3 years after the last date a product model or type was manufactured"
— CA SB-244 (enacted, Required)

Methodology & limits
  • Source. 80 bills across 13 states classified and analyzed by SignalScout; design signals extracted from each bill's compliance text and verified to be verbatim before inclusion (no paraphrased citations).
  • Authority. Enacted laws are current obligations; proposed bills indicate direction and may change or fail. Each principle notes how many of its backing bills are enacted.
  • Coverage. Drawn from the bills with full compliance extractions to date — strong on flagship enacted programs (CA, OR, CO, ME, CT, MN, VT), lighter on early-stage bills.
  • Costs. No fee or cost figures are asserted; eco-modulation rates are set by producer responsibility organizations (e.g. Circular Action Alliance) and are pending. Once published, each design lever maps to fee exposure based on the volume of covered product you sell.
  • This is design guidance, not legal advice. Confirm obligations against the statute and your counsel for the states you operate in.
